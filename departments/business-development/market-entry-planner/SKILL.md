# Market Entry Planner

Design a plan to enter a new market — whether a new geography, a new customer vertical, or a new product segment. Market entry is one of the highest-stakes strategic decisions a portfolio company makes: the cost of a poor market entry (wrong timing, wrong customers, wrong go-to-market) can set a company back 12-24 months. This skill structures the analysis and provides a recommended entry approach with specific milestones and go/no-go criteria.

## When to Use
- Matt Mathison or a portfolio company CEO asks "should we enter [market]?"
- A partnership opportunity has surfaced in a new market and requires an entry strategy to pursue it
- A portfolio company's existing market is saturating — time to think about adjacent markets
- Competitive pressure is pushing toward geographic expansion to protect existing share
- A potential acquisition target is in a new market and the acquisition thesis requires an entry strategy

## Market Entry Decision Framework

```
Market entry requires four answers before committing:

1. Is the market attractive? (Size, growth, competition, margin profile)
2. Do we have the right to win? (Differentiation, existing advantages, credibility)
3. What is the right entry mode? (Direct / Partnership / Acquisition / Trial / License)
4. What does success look like — and how do we know if we're wrong early?

Do NOT invest in market entry without specific answers to all four.
```

## Output Contract
- All four framework questions answered before recommendation — market entry decisions made without assessing both market attractiveness AND right to win are the most common source of failed expansions; both must be scored
- Financial model always includes break-even — "we'll figure out the business case as we go" is not acceptable for a market entry decision; the break-even timeline gives Matt Mathison a specific performance horizon to evaluate against
- 90-day checkpoint always included — market entry plans without a defined go/no-go checkpoint become zombie investments; the checkpoint forces a decision to double down, pivot, or exit at a pre-defined moment
- Partnership entry is usually the right first mode for MBL portfolio companies — at MBL scale and stage, entering a new market via a partner costs 60-80% less than direct entry and carries lower risk; default to partnership entry unless there is a strong reason to go direct
- HITL required: Dr. Lewis builds the analysis; Matt Mathison approves any market entry decision that requires investment >$10K or dedicated BD time >20 hrs/quarter; CEO of the relevant portfolio company is co-decision-maker on their own market entries; legal review if regulatory barriers are identified

## System Dependencies
- **Reads from:** Market research, competitive intelligence, existing customer data, partner ecosystem map, financial model
- **Writes to:** Market entry plan (SharePoint/BD/Market Entry/<Market>); Matt Mathison brief
- **HITL Required:** Dr. Lewis analyzes; Matt Mathison approves entry decision and investment; portfolio company CEO co-decides; legal reviews regulatory barriers

## Test Cases
1. **Golden path:** Allevio considers entering the Colorado independent physician practice market → Market: $180M TAM (est.), 12% growth, 3 established competitors (1 dominant); Attractiveness: 22/30; Right to win: 14/25 (no relationships, no brand, product fits but no local track record); Recommended entry mode: Partnership (2 Colorado healthcare consultants serve 45 practices each); First partner candidate: contacted through AZ Medical Association peer connection; partnership signed within 60 days; first Colorado customer closed at 90 days; Year 1 investment: $8,500 (Dr. Lewis time + 2 partner acquisition costs); Break-even: Month 14; Matt Mathison approved entry on Day 30
2. **Edge case:** The target market appears attractive but requires a state license we don't have → this is a hard stop until the regulatory question is resolved; commission a specific regulatory review before spending BD time on market entry; if the license takes 12-18 months to obtain, partnership entry with a licensed local operator may be the only viable near-term path; document the regulatory barrier and the resolution options clearly for Matt Mathison
3. **Adversarial:** A competitor announces they're expanding into the target market at the same time as our analysis → accelerate the go/no-go decision — don't let competitive FOMO push us into a market that doesn't meet the attractiveness or right-to-win thresholds; if the market is genuinely attractive and we have right to win, move faster; if we're borderline on right to win, the competitor's presence makes this harder, not a reason to rush in; document the competitive development and factor it into the risk register

## Audit Log
Market entry analyses retained. Go/no-go checkpoint outcomes documented. Investment approvals by Matt Mathison logged. Revenue vs. plan tracked quarterly until break-even.

## Deprecation
Retire when portfolio companies have dedicated strategy teams that own market expansion decisions with their own internal analysis frameworks.
