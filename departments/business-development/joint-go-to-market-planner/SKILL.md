# Joint Go-to-Market Planner

Design a joint go-to-market (GTM) plan with a partner — a coordinated strategy where both companies bring their respective strengths to reach a shared target market together. Joint GTM is more than a referral arrangement: it involves shared messaging, joint content, combined customer outreach, and coordinated sales activities. Done right, it multiplies reach without proportionally multiplying cost. Done wrong, it creates confusing customer experiences and internal coordination overhead that kills both teams' productivity.

## When to Use
- A strategic partner is ready to go beyond referrals to active joint marketing
- A new market is being entered where a partner has distribution and we have the solution
- A major industry event is coming up where a joint presence would be more impactful
- Two complementary solutions can be marketed together to a shared audience
- A top-tier (Gold) partner wants a more collaborative relationship

## Joint GTM Design Principles

```
1. One joint message: What's the combined value proposition?
   "We help [customer type] with [their solution] + we help them with [our solution]"
   must be one coherent story, not two separate pitches stapled together.

2. Shared target customer profile: Both companies must agree on exactly who to target.
   If we're targeting enterprise and they're targeting SMB, the GTM will produce
   fragmented messaging and zero joint pipeline.

3. Lead ownership is agreed upfront: When the joint campaign generates a lead,
   who owns it? Whose CRM does it go in? Who makes the first call?
   Don't discover this after the campaign.

4. Budget is proportional to strategic value: The company that benefits more
   from the joint GTM should invest more. Don't split 50/50 by default —
   calculate proportional benefit and split accordingly.

5. Attribution before launch: How will you measure the impact of the joint GTM?
   Define success metrics before spending a dollar.
```

## Output Contract
- Joint value proposition always written in one sentence — if we can't state the joint value in one sentence, the GTM will produce disjointed messaging in market; the one-sentence joint value prop is a forcing function for clarity; it must be agreed by both parties before any campaign activity begins
- Lead routing defined before launch — the most common joint GTM failure mode is two companies running campaigns together and then arguing about who owns the leads; the routing table must be agreed and documented before the first activity
- Attribution window always set — joint GTM attribution is always time-limited; a 12-month attribution window is standard; what happens after 12 months? The attribution rule must be explicit so post-GTM deals are not double-counted
- Budget always proportional and approved — "we'll figure out the budget as we go" is how joint GTMs become expensive and unaccountable; total investment must be approved by Matt Mathison before launch
- HITL required: Dr. Lewis builds and coordinates; Matt Mathison approves total budget before launch; any joint content (co-authored, co-branded) requires CEO review before distribution; lead routing must be documented in writing and signed off by both parties; 90-day checkpoint requires Matt Mathison review

## System Dependencies
- **Reads from:** Partner agreement, both companies' target customer profiles, existing marketing materials, event calendars
- **Writes to:** Joint GTM plan (SharePoint/BD/GTM/<Partner>); campaign performance report; CRM (joint GTM attribution tag)
- **HITL Required:** Dr. Lewis builds; Matt Mathison approves budget; CEO reviews joint content; both parties sign off on lead routing; 90-day checkpoint includes Matt Mathison review

## Test Cases
1. **Golden path:** Allevio + EMR implementation partner launch joint GTM in AZ market → Joint value prop: "Physician practices in Arizona can now migrate their EMR and optimize their billing workflow in a single coordinated implementation, typically reducing billing errors by 35% and EMR adoption friction by 60%"; Joint webinar: "The Modern Practice Tech Stack" — 180 attendees, 12 qualified leads; Co-authored article: published in AZ Medical Association newsletter — 850 reads; Joint email campaign: 200 each from both lists, 7% conversion to call; 90-day results: $145K joint pipeline generated, 3 deals in final stage; ROI: 6.2× on $23K joint investment; Matt Mathison approved continuation for Q4
2. **Edge case:** Partner doesn't deliver on their joint GTM commitments (agreed to co-author a case study, hasn't contributed content in 6 weeks) → address directly on the standing sync: "I want to flag that the case study is 6 weeks behind — we were counting on your contribution for our [event] timeline. What's blocking you?" If they can't deliver, unblock the specific barrier; if they repeatedly fail to deliver, restructure the joint GTM around activities they can actually support; a joint GTM that is 80% us and 20% them is not a joint GTM, it's a sponsored marketing effort
3. **Adversarial:** A competitor approaches the partner after the joint GTM launches and offers them better co-marketing terms → evaluate whether the partner is willing to run joint GTM with multiple companies, including our competitors; if the joint GTM agreement doesn't have a non-compete on joint marketing, we can't prevent it; assess whether the joint GTM should continue if the partner is simultaneously running co-marketing with a competitor in the same market; escalate to Matt Mathison for a strategic decision

## Audit Log
Joint GTM plans retained by partner and period. Campaign activity and results logged. Lead attribution records maintained. Budget approvals documented. 90-day checkpoint outcomes retained.

## Deprecation
Retire when portfolio companies have dedicated partner marketing roles who own joint GTM as a standard partnership activation activity.
