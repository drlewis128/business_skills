# BD Hiring Scorecard

Score a business development or partnerships candidate against the criteria that matter for success in an MBL portfolio company BD role. BD is one of the hardest roles to hire for because great BD people have an external-facing skill set that is difficult to observe in an interview — relationship-building, deal instinct, and follow-through discipline. This scorecard identifies what to look for and how to evaluate it.

## When to Use
- Interviewing a BD, partnerships, or channel development candidate
- Evaluating a candidate referred by Matt Mathison or an advisor
- Scoring candidates after a BD interview loop for a portfolio company
- Deciding between multiple BD finalists
- Setting the criteria before posting a BD role

## What Makes a Great BD Person at MBL Scale

```
MBL portfolio companies need BD people who can:

1. Build trust-based relationships quickly: In a referral-driven model, the BD person IS the
   partnership. They must be genuinely warm, curious, and relationship-first.

2. Drive self-directed prospecting: No one is feeding them a list of partners to call.
   They identify targets, develop outreach, and follow up without being managed.

3. Manage to commitments: Partner programs fail because of poor follow-through.
   Great BD people do what they say they'll do, consistently.

4. Think commercially: They understand the economics of a deal — not just the
   relationship. They know when a partnership is worth pursuing and when it isn't.

5. Represent the company well: Every partner interaction is a brand impression.
   The BD person must be the person you'd be proud to have represent you.

THRIVE hard filter: Integrity is non-negotiable. A BD person who overpromises to close
a partnership, misrepresents the company's capabilities, or hides bad news is a liability.
```

## Interview Questions (full set)

**Relationship building:**
- "Walk me through how you built your most important partnership relationship. How long did it take? What did you invest?"
- "Who is the most influential person in your professional network? How do you maintain that relationship?"

**Prospecting:**
- "Show me how you'd identify the top 10 partnership targets for [Company] in [market] in the next 30 days."
- "What's your best outreach email? Walk me through why it works."

**Commercial:**
- "What partnership economics have you negotiated? What were the key terms?"
- "What's the worst partnership you walked away from? Why was it the right call?"

**Execution:**
- "What's your system for managing partner relationships? Show me your tracker."
- "Tell me about a time a partner was frustrated with you or your company. How did you handle it?"

**THRIVE:**
- "Tell me about a time a partnership wasn't going well. What did you tell your leadership and when?"
- "What would your last partner say is your biggest weakness as a BD person?"

## Reference Check Questions (call 2-3 references)

1. "On a scale of 1-10, how effective is [Candidate] at building trust with partners or clients quickly?"
2. "Describe their follow-through. Can you give a specific example where they came through on a difficult commitment?"
3. "Have you ever seen them overpromise or misrepresent something to close a deal?"
4. "What kind of BD opportunity would they NOT be right for?"
5. "Would you hire them again? Why or why not?"

## Output Contract
- Relationship-building always weighted highest (25 points) — a BD person who can't build relationships is not a BD person regardless of how articulate they are about strategy; the most important skill is the hardest to teach
- Commercial acumen always tested with specific questions — asking "do you understand partnership economics?" produces a "yes" answer; asking them to calculate a specific deal on the spot reveals the truth
- References always called before hiring — self-reported BD excellence is the norm; reference confirmation is what separates truth from narrative; call at least 2 references, ask specifically about overpromising and follow-through, which are the most common BD failure modes
- Integrity hard filter — automatic disqualifier — any BD candidate who shows signs of overpromising, misrepresentation, or hiding bad news should not be hired regardless of relationship skills or commercial acumen; a BD person who overpromises to close partnerships creates liabilities that take months to unwind
- HITL required: Dr. Lewis conducts scoring; for senior BD roles (Director level), Matt Mathison interviews the final candidate before offer; any BD hire >$100K annual compensation requires Matt Mathison approval; references checked by Dr. Lewis; offer letters require CEO signature

## System Dependencies
- **Reads from:** Job description, candidate resume, prior BD scorecard results (for comparison)
- **Writes to:** Hiring scorecard (SharePoint/HR/<Company>/Hiring); Matt Mathison brief for senior roles
- **HITL Required:** Dr. Lewis scores; Matt Mathison interviews senior BD candidates; all offers require CEO approval; no hire with Integrity disqualifier regardless of other scores

## Test Cases
1. **Golden path:** Allevio interviews a Director of BD candidate → Reference-first: candidate referred by a mutual contact (relationship started warmly); Scorecard: Relationship Building 22/25 (strong — references confirm), Prospecting 17/20 (systematic pipeline approach; could improve on LinkedIn prospecting), Commercial 19/20 (excellent — calculated the deal economics on the spot, negotiated 4 partnerships in prior role), Execution 18/20 (references confirm follow-through; one reference noted "occasionally needs reminders on admin tasks"), THRIVE 14/15 (integrity: strong — told a partner when a product wasn't ready even though it lost the deal; hustle: very high); Total: 90/100; Recommendation: Strong hire; Matt Mathison interviewed and agreed; offer extended within 5 business days
2. **Edge case:** Candidate scores well on all dimensions (85/100) but one reference says "they overpromised on a partnership and it cost us a client relationship" → trigger the Integrity hard filter; discuss the specific incident with the candidate in a follow-up call; if they acknowledge it, explain what they learned, and references overall confirm strong integrity, this may not be disqualifying (everyone has a failure); if they deflect blame or minimize it, that's confirmation of the concern; document the decision and rationale either way
3. **Adversarial:** Candidate claims impressive partnership results ($5M in attributable revenue) but provides no specifics when pressed → "Walk me through how that $5M was calculated. Which partners generated the most? How was attribution determined?"; BD candidates who claim large numbers but can't get specific are often inflating; follow up with reference: "Can you verify the partnership revenue this candidate claims — specifically around the $5M number?"; hire on verified facts, not claimed figures

## Audit Log
Hiring scorecards retained by candidate and date. Reference check notes documented. Hiring decisions with rationale retained. Matt Mathison interview outcomes logged. Integrity disqualifiers documented with specific evidence.

## Deprecation
Retire when portfolio companies have dedicated HR/talent teams with their own BD-specific hiring frameworks and structured interview processes that don't require a Dr. Lewis-authored scorecard for each hire.
