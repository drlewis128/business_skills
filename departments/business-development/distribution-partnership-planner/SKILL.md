# Distribution Partnership Planner

Plan a distribution partnership — an arrangement where a partner distributes our product or service to markets or customer segments we can't efficiently reach directly. Distribution partnerships are the highest-scale partnership model: instead of adding one referral partner at a time, a distribution partner reaches hundreds or thousands of potential customers through their existing network, platform, or customer relationships. The trade-off is that distribution partnerships require more infrastructure (enablement, support, quality control) and the company gives up more margin and customer relationship control.

## When to Use
- A company has proven product-market fit but lacks the sales capacity to reach the full addressable market
- A potential distribution partner has a large, relevant audience and is interested in adding our offering
- Entering a new market via a partner who already has distribution in that market
- Evaluating whether to build direct sales capacity or partner for distribution
- A marketplace, platform, or professional network wants to offer our solution to their members

## Distribution Partnership vs. Alternatives

```
Distribution partnership is different from:

Referral: Partner introduces us to individual prospects, then steps back.
          Distribution partner has bulk access and routes customers systematically.

Reseller: Partner buys and resells directly; owns the customer transaction.
          Distribution partner may or may not own the customer transaction — depends on structure.

White-label: Partner sells under their own brand.
             Distribution partner may use either brand depending on structure.

The defining feature of a distribution partnership is SCALE:
the partner has structural access to many customers at once —
through a platform, association, marketplace, or large client base.
```

## Output Contract
- Addressable audience always quantified — "large partner network" is not a distribution opportunity assessment; the number of relevant customers the partner can reach and the expected conversion rate from reach to customer are the two most important variables; both must be estimated
- Infrastructure assessment always included — distribution at scale fails when the company isn't ready to service the volume; the support, onboarding, and billing model must be designed before launch, not after the distribution partner has sent 200 customers
- 90-day soft launch checkpoint always built in — full distribution launch without a pilot creates the risk of quality problems at scale; the 90-day checkpoint with a go/no-go threshold is how early signals are caught before they become large problems
- Non-compete clause always included — a distribution partner who is simultaneously distributing a competitor's product has a conflict that reduces their motivation and potentially your market advantage; the non-compete during the term is standard
- HITL required: Dr. Lewis plans; Matt Mathison approves any distribution agreement with >$25K annual revenue potential or that grants exclusivity; legal reviews all distribution agreements; CEO of the portfolio company co-approves since distribution partnerships affect their go-to-market strategy; any exclusivity clause requires Matt Mathison approval

## Test Cases
1. **Golden path:** Allevio distribution partnership with the Arizona Medical Association (AMA) → AMA endorses Allevio to 800 member physician practices; Model: Association endorsement + referral infrastructure (members visit a co-branded landing page, submit interest, Allevio closes directly); Economics: Allevio pays AMA $500/member converted (vs. 18% referral fee = $720 on $4,000 average monthly contract); net savings of 30% vs. standard referral model; AMA motivated: adds value to membership, generates non-dues revenue; Phase 1: co-branded campaign page (2 weeks); Phase 2: featured in AMA newsletter (Month 1); Phase 3: webinar to members (Month 2); 90-day result: 18 member inquiries, 4 converted, $16K net revenue; Year 1 target: 25 members converted; Matt Mathison approved
2. **Edge case:** Distribution partner requests exclusivity in the Arizona market → evaluate the real cost of exclusivity: how large is the Arizona market? What other distribution partners might we sign that exclusivity would block? What volume commitment is the partner making to "earn" exclusivity? Propose: exclusivity for 12 months if partner achieves [N] customers; exclusivity ends if they fall below [N] customers in any rolling 6-month period; this creates incentive alignment and protects us if the partner underperforms; don't grant perpetual exclusivity without meaningful volume thresholds
3. **Adversarial:** After 8 months, a distribution partner's conversion rate is far below projections (3% vs. 15% projected) → investigate the cause before assuming the model is broken; most common causes: (1) partner isn't actively promoting (fixed with outreach commitment + incentive); (2) the audience isn't as targeted as expected (fixed with better filtering); (3) the offer doesn't resonate with their audience (fixed with message testing); (4) the partner is under-resourced to support the distribution (fixed with co-selling or more enablement); if conversion rate doesn't improve after 90 days of targeted intervention, the distribution model with this specific partner may be structurally unworkable; begin wind-down conversation

## Audit Log
Distribution partnership plans retained. Agreement terms and approvals documented. Launch metrics tracked. 90-day checkpoint decisions retained. Customer conversion and retention tracked by distribution source.

## Deprecation
Retire when portfolio companies have a dedicated channel sales team that manages distribution partnerships as a standard go-to-market function with its own program management infrastructure.
