# White-Label Strategy Advisor

Evaluate and design a white-label strategy — an arrangement where one company's product or service is sold by another company under the latter's brand. White-labeling can dramatically expand distribution with minimal incremental cost, but it comes with significant trade-offs: brand invisibility, customer relationship loss, quality control challenges, and a dependency on the white-label partner's success. This skill structures the strategic analysis and provides specific design guidance for white-label arrangements.

## When to Use
- A potential partner wants to resell our product under their own brand
- We want to use another company's product as the foundation for our own offering
- Evaluating whether white-labeling is better than building internally or partnering through a referral model
- Designing a white-label agreement that protects the company's interests
- A white-label arrangement is being considered as a market entry strategy

## White-Label Role Definitions

```
Two roles in a white-label arrangement:

White-labeler (us selling under another brand): We produce the product/service.
The partner brand it, sells it, and owns the customer relationship.
  Pro: Guaranteed revenue without sales overhead; market reach via partner's brand.
  Con: Invisible to end customer; dependent on partner's performance; 
       no direct relationship to upsell or retain.

White-labelee (us buying and branding someone else's product):
We brand and sell a product we didn't build.
  Pro: Faster to market; no R&D investment; leverage existing technology.
  Con: Dependent on supplier; supplier may compete directly; IP is theirs; 
       if supplier raises prices or exits, we have a product problem.

Most MBL portfolio decisions involve being the white-labeler (selling our capability
under a partner's brand) because our portfolio companies have the technology/service
capability that partners want to offer.
```

## Output Contract
- Economics always explicit — white-label arrangements that don't model the gross margin on the white-label revenue vs. alternatives are being evaluated without the most important data point; the economics table must be complete
- IP analysis always included — the most dangerous white-label arrangement is one where improvements made to the product during the partnership become the partner's IP; this must be explicitly addressed in the agreement
- Change of control clause always required — a white-label partner who is acquired by a competitor is a strategic threat; the change of control clause is non-negotiable
- Transition period always designed — white-label arrangements without a transition period at termination leave partner customers without service; this creates liability and damages the end customer relationship (even if hidden); always design a transition plan
- HITL required: Dr. Lewis builds the analysis; Matt Mathison approves any white-label arrangement; legal must review all white-label agreements (IP, brand, data, liability); CEO of the portfolio company co-approves since it affects their brand strategy; healthcare white-label agreements (Allevio) require HIPAA/compliance counsel before signing

- **Reads from:** Product/service description, cost model, competitive analysis, existing brand guidelines
- **Writes to:** White-label strategy memo (SharePoint/BD/White-Label); agreement draft for legal review
- **HITL Required:** Dr. Lewis analyzes; Matt Mathison approves; legal reviews agreement; portfolio CEO co-approves; healthcare counsel for Allevio

## Test Cases
1. **Golden path:** A regional healthcare consulting firm wants to offer Allevio's RCM under their own brand to their physician practice clients → Economics: consultant buys at $800/practice/month, sells at $950 (18% margin for consultant); our margin: 72% on wholesale; Volume commitment: 10 practices in Year 1 (base case), 25 in Year 2; Agreement: 18-month initial term; change of control clause; quality standards: biannual NPS audit; IP: Allevio retains all IP; modifications: any workflow customizations remain Allevio IP; customer data: Allevio retains aggregate data, no PII without patient consent + BAA; transition period: 180 days; HIPAA/legal review: completed; Matt Mathison approved; first 3 practices onboarded in 45 days; by Month 6: 11 practices (ahead of Year 1 target)
2. **Edge case:** The potential white-label partner is also a competitor in an adjacent service line (they do medical coding, we do billing) → evaluate the conflict explicitly: is there a scenario where their customers migrate from the white-labeled Allevio RCM to their own future billing offering? If yes, the partner has an incentive to build their own billing capability using what they learn from white-labeling ours; this is a common white-label risk ("build vs. partner" decision by the partner); mitigate with non-compete: "Partner may not launch their own competing RCM product during the term and for 24 months after"; escalate to Matt Mathison and legal before proceeding
3. **Adversarial:** After 12 months, the white-label partner demands a lower wholesale price, threatening to switch to a competitor → evaluate the threat: is it credible? (Is there a viable alternative they could realistically switch to?) If not credible, hold firm: the agreement has pricing for the term. If credible, evaluate whether a modest price reduction is worth retaining the volume vs. losing it; escalate to Matt Mathison with a recommendation; never renegotiate under threat without evaluating whether the threat is real; if you concede to every price threat, you train the partner to use it repeatedly

## Audit Log
White-label strategy analyses retained. Agreement terms and approvals documented. IP modification tracking logged. NPS audit results retained. Change of control clause events documented.

## Deprecation
Retire when portfolio companies have a dedicated strategic partnerships function that manages white-label arrangements as a standard licensing/distribution channel.
