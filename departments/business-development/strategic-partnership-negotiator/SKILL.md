# Strategic Partnership Negotiator

Prepare for a partnership negotiation — developing a clear understanding of our position, the partner's likely priorities, the terms that matter most, and the concession strategy that gets us to a signed agreement without giving away unnecessary value. Partnership negotiations differ from sales negotiations: both parties should feel they won, because a partnership where one party feels they conceded everything will produce a disengaged partner who doesn't actively sell or refer. The goal is a deal both parties are motivated to execute.

## When to Use
- A partnership conversation has progressed to the point of discussing specific terms
- A partner has made a proposal and we need to prepare a counter-proposal
- Entering a high-stakes partnership negotiation (strategic alliance, JV, exclusive distribution)
- A partner is pushing for terms we need to evaluate (exclusivity, higher fees, performance guarantees)
- Preparing Matt Mathison or a CEO for a partnership negotiation meeting

## Negotiation Design Principles

```
Five partnership negotiation principles:

1. Know your BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement):
   What's our alternative if this deal doesn't happen? 
   A strong BATNA gives negotiating leverage; a weak one means we're more likely to over-concede.

2. Separate economic terms from relationship terms:
   Partner economics (fee rate, royalties, margins) require Matt Mathison approval.
   Relationship terms (communication, support, marketing) have more flexibility.
   Know which is which before walking in.

3. Lead with value, not price:
   Immediately discussing fee rates frames the negotiation as a cost conversation.
   Lead with: "What makes this partnership valuable to your business?" 
   Understanding their value perception shapes their economics expectations.

4. Don't make unilateral concessions:
   Every concession should be conditional: "We can do X if you can do Y."
   Unilateral concessions signal that you were holding back — they invite more demands.

5. Document everything in the room:
   Decisions made verbally in a negotiation are forgotten differently by each party.
   Capture agreed terms during the meeting, not after.
```

## Opening Strategy

**Open on:** [Which term to discuss first — usually a lower-stakes term to establish collaborative tone before reaching hard terms]

**Opening frame:**
> "Before we get into specific terms, I'd like to understand what a successful partnership looks like from your perspective. What would make this partnership genuinely valuable to [Partner Company] over the first year?"

**Why:** Their answer to this question reveals their priorities and helps us calibrate which terms they care about most. A partner who talks immediately about fee rates has different priorities than one who talks about customer success and co-marketing.

---

## Concession Strategy

**Concession sequence (what to give, in order, and only if necessary):**

1. First concession (give early, low cost): [E.g., "Faster payment terms — from 45 to 30 days"]
2. Second concession (if needed): [E.g., "Fee rate from 18% to 19%"]
3. Third concession (if needed): [E.g., "Fee rate to 20%"]
4. Hard stop: [What we will not concede regardless — e.g., "Exclusivity without volume minimums; fee rate above 22%; 24-month term without performance clause"]

**Every concession is conditional:**
> "We can move to [Y] on [Term] if you can agree to [Counter-term] — does that work?"

---

## Deal-Breakers (automatic escalation to Matt Mathison — do not agree in the meeting)

- [ ] Exclusivity in any form without Matt Mathison approval
- [ ] Fee rate above [X%] without Matt Mathison approval
- [ ] Non-compete that restricts our business outside this partnership
- [ ] Personal liability clause or officer guarantees
- [ ] Contract value >$25K total (standard threshold)
- [ ] Any term that restricts our ability to work with other partners

**Response if they push on a deal-breaker:**
> "That's an important term — I want to make sure I bring you a considered response. Can I take that back to our leadership and get back to you within [48 hours]?"

---

## Post-Negotiation Documentation

At the end of the meeting:
- Verbally summarize all agreed terms
- Send a written term summary within 24 hours
- Terms are not final until written and signed — be clear about this
- Matt Mathison notified of outcome and any open issues within 24 hours

- [ ] Led with value question (not price)
- [ ] Documented agreed terms in real time
- [ ] Did not unilaterally concede — every concession was conditional
- [ ] Did not agree to any deal-breaker terms without Matt Mathison

**After the meeting:**
- [ ] Written term summary sent within 24 hours
- [ ] Matt Mathison briefed
- [ ] Legal engaged if ready for agreement drafting

## Output Contract
- BATNA always assessed — a negotiator who hasn't assessed their BATNA cannot know when to walk away; the BATNA assessment is the most important input to negotiation strategy; it must be done before any terms discussion
- Term-by-term positions always prepared — "we'll see where the conversation goes" is not a negotiation strategy; every material term must have an ideal, acceptable, and walk-away position documented before the meeting
- Deal-breakers flagged and escalation triggers defined — deal-breakers that are agreed in the meeting because the negotiator didn't know to stop and escalate create agreements that can't be honored or that require renegotiation; every deal-breaker must be pre-defined with a specific response script
- Concession strategy conditional — unilateral concessions are almost always a mistake in partnership negotiations; the concession strategy must be conditional ("we can do X if you can do Y") so every move creates a reciprocal obligation
- HITL required: Dr. Lewis prepares and leads negotiations; Matt Mathison briefs before any major partnership negotiation; any deal-breaker term triggers immediate escalation to Matt Mathison; no partnership agreement finalized without Matt Mathison approval; legal drafts all final agreements

## Test Cases
1. **Golden path:** Negotiating a referral agreement with an AZ healthcare consulting firm → Our BATNA: 2 other consulting firms we've identified; Their BATNA: they have no alternative billing partner relationship; Opening: "What would make this partnership genuinely valuable for your consulting clients?"; Their priority revealed: faster payment (they're a small firm with cash flow sensitivity); Their economics ask: 22% (our standard: 18%); Concession sequence: (1) gave 30-day payment vs. our standard 45 (no economic cost to us, high value to them); (2) countered 19% fee ("that's the upper range of what we can do on our standard program"); (3) they accepted 19% with 30-day payment — signed within 48 hours; Matt Mathison briefed same day; agreement drafted by legal within 5 days
2. **Edge case:** Partner asks for a personal guarantee from the company's CEO on the partnership agreement → this is an unusual and inappropriate ask for a referral partnership; redirect: "Our company is the party to the partnership agreement — we don't include personal guarantees in partnership arrangements"; if they persist, this is a deal-breaker; escalate to Matt Mathison; a company asking for a personal guarantee on a referral agreement is either confused about the deal structure or has an ulterior motive; investigate before proceeding
3. **Adversarial:** Partner comes to the negotiation having already had a lawyer draft a one-sided agreement and presents it as "our standard terms" → don't be pressured by the existence of a draft; review carefully; identify every term that deviates from your positions; present a redline or counter-term sheet; do not sign "their standard terms" without review just because they were presented first; the party who prepares the first draft gains a psychological advantage but not a legal one; their "standard" is their opening position, not a fixed point

## Audit Log
Negotiation prep documents retained. Post-meeting term summaries retained. Final agreed terms documented. Matt Mathison briefing outcomes logged. Concession history tracked (what was given and for what in return).

## Deprecation
Retire when portfolio companies have experienced BD executives who manage partnership negotiations as a standard function with their own negotiation frameworks and legal support.
