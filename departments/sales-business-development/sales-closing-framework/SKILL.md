# Closing Framework

Close sales deals and advance stalled negotiations for MBL portfolio companies — giving reps the structure and language to ask for the business without being pushy and to create urgency without being manipulative. Closing is not a trick; it is the natural conclusion of a well-run sales process. A rep who has done excellent discovery and built a compelling proposal should close naturally; a rep who is afraid to close has either not built enough value or is afraid of rejection.

## When to Use
- A deal has been in the Proposal stage for more than 14 days without forward movement
- A rep is preparing for the "ask for the business" conversation
- A prospect keeps delaying ("let me get back to you next month")
- A deal needs urgency without a fake deadline

## Closing Framework

```
CLOSING PRINCIPLES:
  Close early and often — small commitments build toward the big one
    "Can we put a 30-minute call on the calendar for next Thursday to walk through the proposal?"
    "Does this seem like the right direction for your organization?"
    "If the numbers work out, is there any reason we wouldn't move forward?"
  
  The close is not the end of a presentation — it is a question
    Every significant sales meeting should end with a specific next step: date; time; action
    "Next step" is not a close — "Will you sign by Friday?" is a close
    
  Create urgency from reality, not from fake deadlines:
    Fake: "This pricing is only available until Friday." (They know it's not true)
    Real: "Your open enrollment is October 1. Implementation takes 30-60 days. If we sign
      by August 15, we have time to complete implementation before your employees need to use
      the plan. If we slip past August 15, the January 1 start date is at risk."
      
CLOSE TYPES — BY SITUATION:

  1. TRIAL CLOSE (test the temperature; not a final ask):
    Use when: Discovery is complete; the prospect seems interested; you want to confirm before
      building a proposal
    Language:
      "Based on what we've talked about — does this seem like a fit for what you're trying
        to solve?"
      "If the numbers come back where you need them, is there any reason we wouldn't move
        forward?"
    Listen for: "Yes, send me the proposal" (green light); "I'd need to talk to my CFO" (normal
      next step — not a no); "I'm not sure" (still has an unresolved concern — explore it)
      
  2. SUMMARY CLOSE (restate the value; then ask):
    Use when: After the proposal presentation; the prospect has heard the full case
    Language:
      "So — we've talked about [pain]; we've shown you how Allevio addressed it for [similar
        company]; we've shown that at $85 PMPM you'd save $[X] annually vs. your current plan.
        Does this feel like the right decision for your team?"
    Listen for: "Yes, what are the next steps?" (close it); "I need to think about it" (use
      the exploration framework from sales-objection-handler — don't schedule a follow-up
      without understanding what they're thinking about)
      
  3. NEXT STEP CLOSE (advance the deal with specificity):
    Use when: The deal is moving but not closing yet; you need a concrete forward commitment
    Language:
      "I'll have the contract over to you by Wednesday. Who on your end needs to review it
        before it's signed — is that just you or does your CFO need to review?"
      "I'll have the IO ready by Thursday. Who should I copy on that?"
    Listen for: Who they name as the reviewer (important stakeholder information); any surprise
      step in the review process (is there a legal team review they didn't mention?)
      
  4. URGENCY CLOSE (create real urgency; not artificial):
    Use when: The deal is stalled and there is a real deadline
    Language:
      Allevio: "I don't want to create pressure, but I want to make sure you know: your
        open enrollment starts October 1. Implementation takes 30-60 days. If we sign by
        August 15, I can guarantee your employees have access on Day 1. If we push into
        September, I can't guarantee the January 1 start date."
      Column6: "The Q3 inventory for premium CTV in [your category] is filling up. I have
        availability now — if the IO slides another 2 weeks, I can't guarantee the placement
        positions your buyers want for the campaign."
      HIVE: "We're actively working with [N] landowners in this area. We have capital committed
        to close [X] additional leases before year-end. I want to make sure you have the
        opportunity to participate in this round before we've committed the last of the allocated
        capital."
        
  5. RECOVERY CLOSE (a stalled deal needs a direct conversation):
    Use when: Deal has had no movement for 30+ days
    Language:
      "I want to check in directly — last time we talked you said [next step]. It's been
        [N] weeks. Is this still a priority for you, or has something changed?"
    Two honest outcomes:
      (a) Still a priority — get a real next step with a date
      (b) Not a priority — better to know now than to continue working a dead deal
      
    Never: Keep sending follow-up emails without a direct conversation (wastes time; damages
      the relationship)

DEAL STALL DIAGNOSTIC:
  If a deal stalls after the proposal stage, ask these questions:
  □ Do we have access to the actual decision-maker? (Or are we working through a champion?)
  □ Is the compelling event real? (What happens if they don't decide? Is there a real cost?)
  □ Is there a hidden stakeholder blocking the decision? (Who hasn't been in the conversation?)
  □ Is the budget confirmed? (Or is it still "we'll find it if we like the solution"?)
  □ Are we actually the preferred vendor? (Or are they using us as a price check vs. a competitor?)
```

## Output Contract
- Every close must have a real next step — a closing conversation that ends with "sounds good — I'll follow up" is not a close; it is a polite deferral; Dr. Lewis trains reps that every closing conversation ends with either (a) a signed contract; (b) a specific date, action, and name attached to the next step; or (c) a clear qualification/disqualification decision; "I'll think about it and get back to you" is not an acceptable outcome from a closing conversation without exploring what they're thinking about
- The urgency must be real — artificial urgency ("pricing expires Friday") destroys trust when the prospect can test it by asking for the same pricing the following Monday; Dr. Lewis builds urgency only from real events (open enrollment deadlines; inventory availability; implementation timelines; budget cycles); a rep who creates fake urgency trains the prospect to distrust them — and loses the deal later even if they keep the conversation alive now
- Stalled deals need a direct conversation, not more emails — a deal that hasn't moved in 30 days will not move from another follow-up email; Dr. Lewis requires reps to have a direct "where are we?" conversation on any deal stalled for 30+ days; the conversation has only two good outcomes: a real next step or a qualification decision (the deal is dead, which is also valuable information); keeping zombie deals alive wastes time and distorts the pipeline
- HITL required: Reps execute closing conversations; Dr. Lewis reviews stalled deals in every pipeline meeting and recommends closing strategies; CEO joins closing conversations for deals >$250K or for at-risk situations; proposals >$50K require Dr. Lewis review before submission and before the closing conversation; Dr. Lewis does not close on behalf of reps — the rep is the primary relationship owner; Dr. Lewis coaches the strategy and may join for executive-level support

## Test Cases
1. **Golden path:** Allevio deal in Proposal stage for 12 days; prospect is the HR Director; budget is confirmed; renewal is October 1; implementation takes 45 days → Dr. Lewis prepares the closing strategy: Urgency close — real deadline is August 15 (October 1 - 45 days implementation); language: "I want to flag a real timeline issue before we go further. Your renewal is October 1. Our implementation takes 45 days from signature to employee access. That puts the latest signature date at August 15. We're at July 18 right now — you have 4 weeks to make this decision without risking your January 1 start date for members. I'm not trying to create pressure — I want to make sure you have the full picture." Rep delivers this in the closing call; HR Director says "I hadn't done that math — I'll talk to my CFO today and get back to you by Friday"; Rep confirms a specific follow-up: "I'll expect your call Friday. If I haven't heard by 2pm I'll give you a quick call." Deal closes following Monday
2. **Edge case:** A Column6 IO for $85K has been in negotiation for 37 days — the agency keeps asking for "one more revision" to the placement plan → Dr. Lewis advises the recovery close: "This deal is stalling. Every revision request is a signal that something isn't resolved. Before the next call, I want you to ask directly: 'I want to make sure I'm not missing something — we've revised the plan 3 times and I want to understand what's holding us back from finalizing. Is it the inventory mix, the CPM, or something else entirely?' If they name a specific issue, solve it. If they can't name a specific issue, it's possible they're using us as a price check for their incumbent DSP. Ask directly: 'Are we competing against someone specific for this budget?' The answer will tell you whether this is a real deal or a negotiation tactic." Rep asks; agency reveals they're getting a counter-proposal from Google DV360; Column6 strengthens the measurement story and the deal closes at 93% of original price
3. **Adversarial:** A rep uses a fake deadline close: "Our pricing changes next Friday — this is the last week we can offer $85/PMPM" (the pricing doesn't change Friday) → Dr. Lewis: "This is a serious problem. We don't have a pricing change next Friday. If the prospect calls us on this (and they will — they can simply ask us the following Monday), we've permanently damaged our credibility. Remove this from your closing language immediately. Real urgency closes only. If you're not sure what the real urgency is for this deal, we'll build it together from the discovery notes. Manufacturing urgency is a short-term tactic that creates long-term trust problems." Rep corrected; pipeline review confirms no other fake urgency language is in use; coaching session scheduled
