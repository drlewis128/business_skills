# Exec Leadership Coaching

Prepare structured leadership coaching frameworks and session materials for MBL portfolio company executives — helping Matt Mathison invest in the professional development of entity CEOs and key leaders in a way that is specific, candid, and growth-oriented. Leadership coaching at MBL is not a soft HR function — it is a core value creation lever. An entity CEO who grows 20% as a leader this year creates compounding returns for the portfolio. Coaching without structure is good intentions that produce inconsistent outcomes. Coaching with a THRIVE framework and specific skill targets produces leaders who can execute without constant intervention.

## When to Use
- Monthly 1-on-1 between Matt Mathison and an entity CEO (prepare the agenda and coaching focus)
- Quarterly leadership review (what's working; what needs development; what's next)
- A specific leadership gap is identified in portfolio review or Krista transcript analysis
- An entity CEO is being prepared for a higher-complexity role (M&A leadership; board communication)
- Dr. Lewis is coaching a team lead or department head within an entity

## Leadership Coaching Framework

```
THRIVE LEADERSHIP DEVELOPMENT MODEL:

  TRUTH — The leader sees reality clearly and communicates it honestly
    Observable behaviors:
      □ Reports bad news as quickly as good news
      □ Doesn't filter data before presenting to Matt Mathison
      □ Runs effective retrospectives after misses (not blame-first post-mortems)
      □ Updates forecasts proactively when assumptions change
    Development lever: "What's the hardest truth about your team or business that you
      haven't fully surfaced to Matt yet? Let's talk about why."
      
  HUSTLE — The leader creates execution velocity
    Observable behaviors:
      □ Decisions are made at the right level without escalation delay
      □ The team is not waiting on the leader to unblock progress
      □ Action items close on schedule (exec-action-tracker completion rate)
      □ The leader is proactive with Matt Mathison, not reactive
    Development lever: "What's one thing you've been overthinking or delayed deciding?
      Let's stress-test it for 10 minutes and make the call."
      
  RESPECT — The leader creates psychological safety and team trust
    Observable behaviors:
      □ Candid feedback is delivered constructively
      □ Difficult conversations are not avoided
      □ Team members feel heard, even when the answer is no
      □ The leader resolves conflict; doesn't let it fester
    Development lever: "Tell me about the most difficult conversation you've had with
      a team member this quarter. How did it go? What would you do differently?"
      
  INTEGRITY — The leader does what they say
    Observable behaviors:
      □ Commitments to Matt Mathison are honored or proactively renegotiated
      □ Commitments to their team are honored
      □ No gap between what the leader says in meetings and what actually happens
      □ Conflicts of interest are disclosed proactively
    Development lever: "What commitments have you made this quarter that you're at
      risk of missing? How are we going to handle them?"
      
  VALUE — The leader is creating measurable returns
    Observable behaviors:
      □ Leader can articulate their entity's contribution to portfolio NRR
      □ Investment decisions are evaluated with ROI discipline
      □ The leader identifies resource waste and eliminates it
      □ The leader is building team capability, not just personal capability
    Development lever: "If we measured the ROI of your time this quarter —
      what had the highest leverage? What had the lowest? What changes?"
      
  ENJOY — The leader is engaged and sustainable
    Observable behaviors:
      □ The leader is energized by the work, not depleted by it
      □ Burnout risk is proactively managed
      □ The leader is developing successors (not building dependency on themselves)
      □ There is something specific the leader enjoys about this role
    Development lever: "What do you enjoy most about this role right now?
      What's draining you? Let's make sure the balance is sustainable."

COACHING SESSION STRUCTURE (Monthly; 60 minutes):

  0:00-0:05 — PERSONAL CHECK-IN:
    One personal question. Not task-related. "How are you doing? Really?"
    Purpose: build trust; catch early burnout signals; model the behavior of genuine care
    
  0:05-0:20 — WHAT'S WORKING:
    Not just results — behaviors. "Tell me something you did this month that worked well."
    Purpose: reinforce good leadership behavior; acknowledge competence
    
  0:20-0:45 — DEVELOPMENT FOCUS:
    One THRIVE dimension as the monthly focus (rotate through all 6 over 6 months)
    Use the development lever for that dimension (see above)
    Specific situation + behavior observation + invitation to reflect
    NOT: "You need to be more X." YES: "I noticed [specific situation]. Walk me through
      your thinking. What would you do differently if you had it to do again?"
      
  0:45-0:55 — FORWARD COMMITMENT:
    One concrete development action the leader commits to this month
    Must be observable and specific: "I will have the hard conversation with [person]
      about [topic] by [date] and I'll tell you how it went in our next call."
    Logged in exec-action-tracker.
    
  0:55-1:00 — CLOSE:
    What did the leader take away from this session?
    Reinforce one specific strength the leader demonstrated in the conversation.

COACHING DOCUMENTATION:

  Session summary written by Dr. Lewis after each session:
    □ Which THRIVE dimension was the focus?
    □ What specific situation was discussed?
    □ What was the leader's insight or reflection?
    □ What is the forward commitment?
    □ What did Dr. Lewis observe about the leader's readiness, engagement, or gaps?
    
  Session summaries: confidential between Dr. Lewis and Matt Mathison.
  NOT shared with the leader being coached unless Dr. Lewis and Matt decide sharing
    would serve the leader's development.
```

## Output Contract
- Coaching is specific, not prescriptive — telling an entity CEO "you need to communicate better" is not coaching; it is a complaint; coaching asks a specific question about a specific situation and invites the leader to reflect on their own behavior; Dr. Lewis prepares the specific situation (from portfolio review, Krista transcripts, or Matt's direct observation) and the non-accusatory opening question that creates the coaching moment; the leader's insight is more durable than Dr. Lewis's instruction
- Commitments must be specific and due-dated — every coaching session ends with one specific commitment from the leader; "I'll work on this" is not a commitment; "I'll have the conversation with [person] about [topic] by [date] and I'll report back" is a commitment; Dr. Lewis logs all coaching commitments in exec-action-tracker and checks in before the next session
- Session summaries are confidential — coaching sessions are trust-based; session summaries stay between Dr. Lewis and Matt Mathison unless all parties agree to share; Dr. Lewis does not share coaching observations with the entity's board, other entity CEOs, or MBL staff without explicit Matt Mathison authorization; the coaching relationship depends on the leader trusting that the space is safe for candor
- HITL required: Matt Mathison defines the THRIVE development priorities for each entity CEO; Dr. Lewis prepares the session framework and presents to Matt before each session (for entity CEO sessions that Matt facilitates directly) or runs independently (for sessions Dr. Lewis facilitates); forward commitments logged in exec-action-tracker; coaching session summaries retained confidentially in executive personnel files

## Test Cases
1. **Golden path:** Allevio CEO has been making good operational progress but Matt has noticed she's slow to surface problems early (Truth dimension gap); Dr. Lewis prepares: Focus: T (Truth); Specific situation from last month's Krista transcript: "In the Allevio CEO call on May 15, the claim ratio had already hit 91% by the time it was raised in the call — it had been trending up for 3 weeks. The CEO had the data." Opening question: "Walk me through what you were thinking as you watched the claim ratio trend in early May — what made you wait to bring it to Matt?" Session is excellent; CEO insight: "I was waiting until I had a solution before bringing the problem — I realize that's backwards." Commitment: "Next time I see a metric trending 3+ days against target, I'll send a 2-sentence heads-up to Matt without waiting for the solution." Logged. Next month: CEO actually sends a 2-sentence early-warning on a different metric. Matt: "Perfect example of what we were working on."
2. **Edge case:** The entity CEO is resistant to coaching and frames it as "micromanagement" → Dr. Lewis: "Let me be direct with you: Matt Mathison invests in leadership coaching because he believes you have the capability to be exceptional in this role. That's the frame. This isn't oversight — it's investment. But I take the feedback seriously. What about our current sessions feels like oversight rather than development? If there's something about the format or the focus that isn't working for you, I want to hear it." CEO: "It's the Integrity questions — they feel like I'm being accused of something." Dr. Lewis: "That's useful. Let's reframe the Integrity dimension: instead of asking what commitments you're at risk of missing, I'll ask what systems you've built to make sure your commitments to Matt always land on time. Same outcome — very different question." CEO: "That works better."
3. **Adversarial:** Matt asks Dr. Lewis to share the coaching session summary with the entity's board chair without the CEO's knowledge → Dr. Lewis: "I'd recommend against that approach. If the board chair learns about the coaching content without the CEO knowing, it breaks the trust that makes the coaching work. If there's something from the coaching sessions that the board should know, I think the right move is either: (1) Matt raises it directly with the CEO first, then with the board; or (2) Matt informs the board using the portfolio review language rather than the coaching session content. What is it that the board chair needs to know? Let's find the right channel for that information."
