# Data Storytelling Guide

Present financial data clearly and compellingly for non-finance audiences — translating accounting outputs into the business narrative that CEOs, board members, and LPs need to make decisions. Financial data without context is noise. Dr. Lewis is responsible for making sure that every financial deliverable — board package, LP report, CEO summary — communicates clearly to the intended audience, leads with the bottom line, explains what the numbers mean (not just what they are), and drives toward a decision or action.

## When to Use
- Preparing a board financial package — structuring the narrative and visual presentation
- A CEO or operator is preparing a presentation to investors or board members
- Dr. Lewis is presenting financial results and needs to frame them for a non-finance audience
- A variance needs to be explained in plain language (not accounting language)
- Matt Mathison asks Dr. Lewis to make a financial summary "easier to understand"

## Financial Storytelling Framework

```
CORE PRINCIPLES OF FINANCIAL STORYTELLING:

  1. BLUF — Bottom Line Up Front:
     Lead with the answer, not the analysis.
     Wrong: "Revenue was $1.21M, which represents an increase of $285K vs. the prior year period,
             equating to a 30.8% growth rate, driven primarily by..."
     Right: "Allevio grew 31% vs. prior year — ahead of the 25% deal model. Strong performance."
     The audience already knows the context; give them the verdict first.
     
  2. One Number Per Slide (for board presentations):
     Each slide should make ONE point.
     Put the single key number in large font; everything else supports it.
     If a slide has 12 numbers, the audience will read 12 numbers and understand zero of them.
     
  3. So What? (always answer this):
     After every financial data point, the audience is asking "so what?"
     The answer: "This means X for us going forward" / "This requires action Y" / "This confirms we're on track"
     Never present a number without its business implication.
     
  4. Explain the WHY before the WHAT:
     Wrong: "EBITDA declined $68K vs. prior quarter."
     Right: "WTI dropped to $63.50 — that's what drove the $68K EBITDA decline. Not a business problem; a commodity market event."
     The audience understands the "what" from the number. They need you to explain the "why."
     
  5. THRIVE standard — truth first, no sugarcoating:
     Bad news is better presented clearly than softened.
     A variance that is presented clearly ("EBITDA is $85K below plan because WTI dropped unexpectedly — here is how we're managing it") is received better than a variance buried in caveats and qualifications.
     Boards and LPs want to know management sees reality clearly.
     
AUDIENCE TRANSLATION GUIDE:

  For CEOs (operational focus):
    Skip: GAAP accounting details, depreciation/amortization mechanics, revenue recognition nuance
    Emphasize: Revenue, EBITDA, cash balance, operating metrics (members/BOE/campaigns)
    Frame: "Here's how the business performed vs. the plan — and here's what we do next"
    Language: Business language, not accounting language (say "profit" not "EBITDA" when appropriate)
    
  For Board Members (governance focus):
    Skip: Day-to-day operational details; granular line-item analysis
    Emphasize: Performance vs. deal model; return trajectory; risk flags; strategic decisions needed
    Frame: "Are we on track? Where are we off track? What decisions does the board need to make?"
    Language: Investment language — multiples, IRR, EBITDA margin, deal model vs. actual
    
  For LPs (investor focus):
    Skip: Operational detail; internal management debates; company-specific granularity beyond the summary
    Emphasize: Portfolio-level performance; fund returns (TVPI, DPI, IRR); company trajectory
    Frame: "Your capital is deployed in these businesses; here is how the investment is performing"
    Language: Fund language — net IRR, MOIC, DPI, RVPI, TVPI; "unrealized" always labeled clearly
    
  For Operations Teams (execution focus):
    Skip: Investor return metrics; fund-level aggregation; abstract financial ratios
    Emphasize: The metric they control (members, BOE, campaigns); their cost budget; their cash
    Frame: "Here is what the number means for your team's operating decisions this month"
    Language: Operational language — "members," "campaigns," "barrels" — not "PMPM" or "EBITDA"
    
NARRATIVE STRUCTURE FOR FINANCIAL PRESENTATIONS:

  Opening sentence: "[Entity] [performed well / is on track / needs attention] — [one-sentence verdict]"
  Context: "Revenue of $[X] was [X]% vs. prior year / vs. plan."
  Bridge: "The key driver of [performance / variance] was [specific factor]."
  Implication: "This means [specific business implication — decision, risk, opportunity]."
  Action: "The action we are taking / recommending is [specific next step]."
  
CHART AND VISUAL GUIDANCE:
  Use waterfall charts for: EBITDA bridges (budget-to-actual, prior period bridge)
  Use bar charts for: Period-over-period comparison (monthly revenue, quarterly EBITDA)
  Use line charts for: Trends over time (member count trajectory, WTI price)
  Use tables for: Detailed data that must be precise (covenant compliance, LP return metrics)
  Use traffic lights for: Status dashboards (portfolio company health, covenant status)
  Avoid: Pie charts (rarely useful for financial data); 3D charts; any chart with >7 data series
  Color guide: Green = good/above target; Amber/Yellow = watch/near threshold; Red = concern/below threshold
  One consistent color palette per presentation — do not mix random colors
  
COMMON FINANCIAL TRANSLATION PAIRS:
  "EBITDA" → "Operating profit before interest, taxes, and depreciation" or simply "operating profit"
  "Variance" → "Difference from plan"
  "LTM" → "Last 12 months"
  "PMPM" → "Monthly fee per member" or "revenue per member per month"
  "LOE" → "Production cost per barrel"
  "DSO" → "How quickly we collect from customers (days)"
  "DPO" → "How long we take to pay suppliers (days)"
  "MOIC" → "Return multiple on our investment"
  "IRR" → "Annual return rate on the investment"
  "TVPI" → "Total value per dollar invested"
  "Contribution margin" → "Revenue minus direct costs — what's left to cover overhead and profit"
```

```markdown

## Opening Statement

[Entity] [performance verdict — e.g., "delivered its strongest quarter"] — [one sentence summary].

- **[Metric 1]:** [Value] — [What this means in plain language]
- **[Metric 2]:** [Value] — [What this means in plain language]
- **[Metric 3]:** [Value] — [What this means in plain language — the risk or watch item]

| Metric | Value | vs. [Plan/Prior Year] | So What? |
|--------|-------|---------------------|---------|
| [Revenue] | $[X]M | +[X]% | [Implies X for the exit model / confirms growth trajectory] |

---

## Explanation of Key Variance (plain language)

[If anything is materially different from plan:]
"[Metric] came in [X]% [above/below] plan — here's why: [plain language explanation of root cause].
This is [a temporary situation / a structural issue that requires action].
[We are managing it by / The board's input is needed on] [specific action]."

---

## Decision / Next Steps for This Audience

[For board:] The board is asked to [approve / note / provide guidance on] [specific item].
[For CEO:] The action for this week is [specific operational action].
[For LP:] No action required — this is provided as portfolio update.
```

## Output Contract
- Never bury the bottom line — every financial communication leads with the answer; if revenue is ahead of plan, say that first; if EBITDA is below plan, say that first with the reason; a financial presentation that makes the audience search through 12 slides to find whether performance is good or bad is structured for the analyst who built it, not the decision-maker who needs to act; Dr. Lewis leads with the verdict in the first sentence of every financial communication
- Every number needs a "so what" — a number without context is data; a number with context is information; a number with context and implication is a decision-ready communication; EBITDA of $852K says nothing to a board member; "EBITDA of $852K is 7.5% ahead of the deal model — at current trajectory, we will reach the Year 3 exit target a quarter early" says everything; Dr. Lewis always adds the "so what" before ending any financial communication
- Different audiences need different translations — a board member who was a surgeon doesn't need a definition of EBITDA, but they don't need three pages of line-item variance analysis either; they need to know: are we on track for the exit multiple we underwrote at acquisition?; Dr. Lewis adapts the presentation to the audience — same underlying data, different framing, different level of granularity, different language; a great financial communicator is one who can present the same results in five different ways for five different audiences without changing a single underlying number
- HITL required: Dr. Lewis structures and writes; the CEO and Matt Mathison review before the presentation is delivered to the board or LPs; any financial data presented to external parties (board members, LPs) requires Matt Mathison review; Dr. Lewis writes the narrative — Matt Mathison presents it; Dr. Lewis does not present to the board or LPs directly without Matt Mathison

## Test Cases
1. **Golden path:** Board meeting next week — EBITDA is $68K below plan due to WTI decline (HIVE) → Dr. Lewis drafts the board narrative for the HIVE section: "HIVE is 18% below EBITDA plan for the quarter — driven entirely by WTI pricing, not operations. Here's the picture: WTI averaged $63.50 in the quarter vs. the $67 plan assumption ($3.50/bbl below plan × 90,000 BOE produced = $315K revenue shortfall → $68K EBITDA shortfall). Production is on plan. Costs are on plan. The business is performing as expected — the commodity market moved against us. At current WTI ($64.50, early Q4), HIVE is tracking to recover in the next quarter if WTI holds. The stress protocol trigger is $48/bbl — we're $16 above it. No action required at current levels." Matt Mathison reviews; approves the narrative; presents to the board. Board asks no follow-up questions because the explanation is pre-emptively clear
2. **Edge case:** LP sends a question asking "why did the portfolio EBITDA decline?" (in response to the quarterly LP report) → Dr. Lewis drafts the LP response for Matt Mathison's review: "Portfolio EBITDA for Q3 came in at $1.13M vs. $1.21M in Q2 — a $78K sequential decline. The decline is entirely attributable to HIVE Partners: WTI oil prices averaged $63.50 in Q3 vs. $67.00 in Q2. At HIVE's current production rate, each $1.00 change in WTI equals approximately $25K of EBITDA. The $3.50 WTI decline equals approximately $87K of EBITDA impact on HIVE, which more than explains the portfolio decline. Allevio and Column6 both posted sequential EBITDA gains. HIVE operations are performing to plan. Current WTI ($64.50) is recovering. No operational issues have been identified at any portfolio company." Matt Mathison reviews; responds to the LP directly using this language
3. **Adversarial:** A board member interrupts the presentation to ask Dr. Lewis directly: "Can you explain the revenue recognition policy for Allevio in detail?" and Matt Mathison is present → Dr. Lewis responds briefly (this is a board meeting, not a technical seminar): "For Allevio, we recognize revenue monthly as services are delivered to members — so each month's revenue equals the enrolled member count times the monthly fee per member. We don't book revenue until it's earned, and we maintain a deferred revenue balance for any pre-paid amounts. The policy is fully consistent with standard accounting practices for subscription-based healthcare services. Happy to provide more detail offline if it would be helpful." Dr. Lewis keeps it accessible; keeps it brief; does not lecture the board on ASC 606 for 10 minutes; offers a deeper offline follow-up for anyone who wants it; Matt Mathison moves the meeting forward
