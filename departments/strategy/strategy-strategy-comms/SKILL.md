# Strategy Communications

Plan and draft internal communications for MBL portfolio companies — communicating strategic plans, key decisions, organizational changes, and acquisitions to internal stakeholders in a way that builds alignment and preserves trust. Communication is the most underestimated strategic capability in small PE portfolio companies: a clear strategy that is never communicated becomes a misunderstood strategy; a good decision that is poorly announced becomes a morale problem; an acquisition that employees learn about from LinkedIn before leadership tells them directly creates the exact kind of talent retention risk that derails integration. At PE portfolio scale, Dr. Lewis treats communication as a strategic act, not an administrative one.

## When to Use
- Announcing a major strategic decision or direction change to the team
- Communicating an acquisition (Day 1 integration communication)
- A leadership change at the company
- Annual strategy — communicating next year's plan and priorities
- A difficult operational decision that affects employees (restructuring, role change)
- Matt Mathison asks how to communicate something sensitive internally

## Strategy Communication Framework

```
Communication principles (small PE portfolio context):

  TIMING:
    Employees should hear from leadership before they hear from anywhere else
    Social media, LinkedIn, local press — all move faster than internal plans assume
    Rule: If it's news, employees hear it from the CEO before it's public
    Day 1 for acquisitions: All-hands announcement the same morning the deal closes
    
  TRANSPARENCY:
    Small companies: Employees know more than leadership assumes
    "We'll share more when we can" is a trust-destroyer when the reality leaks anyway
    Be direct about what you can share and honest about what you can't — yet
    "I can't share the full details until [condition] — when that's done, I'll tell you everything" is acceptable
    "Trust me on this" with no context is not acceptable
    
  BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front):
    State the decision or news first; context and rationale follow
    Employees who hear context before the decision spend the context wondering what the conclusion is
    Wrong: "As we've been growing and thinking about the future of Allevio..."
    Right: "We've made a decision that will shape how we grow over the next three years. [The decision.] Here's what that means for you."
    
  MESSAGE ARCHITECTURE for major announcements:
    1. The decision / news (BLUF — 1-2 sentences)
    2. Why this decision / what led to it (context — 2-3 sentences)
    3. What this means for you / the team (direct answer to the question everyone is asking — 2-3 sentences)
    4. What stays the same (stability — 1-2 sentences; most people fear change will impact them more than it will)
    5. What happens next / timeline (next steps — 1-2 sentences)
    6. How to get more information / ask questions (channel — 1 sentence)
    
  AUDIENCES at MBL portfolio companies:
    All-staff: Major decisions; acquisitions; leadership changes; annual direction
    Manager/leadership team: Advance briefing before all-staff (managers should know before their teams)
    Specific teams: Operational decisions affecting a specific function
    External (customers/patients): Separate from internal; strategy-partnership-evaluator covers external
    
  CHANNEL SELECTION:
    In-person or video (all-hands): Major decisions; anything that could create fear or uncertainty
      Why: Body language; Q&A; CEO or leadership personally present; signals importance
    Email (written): Follow-up to in-person; reference document; lower-stakes updates
    Written + in-person for major decisions: Both channels; email is the record, in-person is the signal
    
  DIFFICULT COMMUNICATIONS:
    Layoffs / role eliminations: In-person (or video for distributed teams); no email layoffs; never
    Acquisition: In-person all-hands; CEO + leadership of both entities present together
    Leadership change (CEO departure): CEO announces their own departure if possible; successor or board announces if not
    Performance issues affecting the team: CEO speaks to team about standards, not about the individual
    
  THRIVE in communications:
    Truth: Say what's true; avoid spin
    Hustle: Don't delay difficult communications; move with urgency
    Respect: Treat employees as adults who can handle honest information
    Integrity: The communication is the message; leaders who say one thing and do another lose credibility with the communication itself
    Value: Does this communication help employees understand how to create value in this direction?
    Enjoy: Even difficult communications can be delivered with genuine care for the team
```

## Message Architecture

**[Title: e.g., "Allevio and Sonoran Health — Joining Forces"]**

### [Paragraph 1 — BLUF: The decision]

"I want to share exciting news: Allevio is joining forces with Sonoran Health, a three-provider primary care practice in Tucson. As of today, they're part of our team."

### [Paragraph 2 — Why]

"This move is consistent with our strategy: bring Allevio's model of accessible, high-quality primary care to more patients across Arizona. Tucson is a market we've been watching for two years. Sonoran Health has the right clinical team, the right patient culture, and the right approach to care — they're exactly the kind of practice we want to grow alongside."

### [Paragraph 3 — What this means for you]

"For the Allevio Phoenix team, this changes nothing about your day-to-day. Your role, your patients, your schedule — all unchanged. What this does mean is that our team is growing. Over the next few months, you'll meet the Sonoran Health team as we connect the two practices and share what we've learned."

### [Paragraph 4 — What stays the same]

"Our culture, our commitment to same-day access, our focus on patient relationships — those don't change. Allevio's model works because of the people in it. That doesn't change."

### [Paragraph 5 — What happens next]

"In the next two weeks, we'll hold a joint call with both teams. You'll hear directly from the Sonoran Health providers about who they are and what they're excited about. [Date] — save the time on your calendar."

### [Paragraph 6 — Questions]

"If you have questions before then, come to me directly. I'd rather you hear the answer from me than read something online that may not be accurate. My door is open."

## Output Contract
- The CEO delivers difficult communications in person — no announcement that affects employees significantly (acquisition, leadership change, significant operational change) is communicated by email first; in-person (or live video for distributed teams) signals that the leadership takes the moment seriously; an email layoff or an email acquisition announcement tells employees that leadership would rather not face the reaction; Dr. Lewis designs all major communications for in-person or live delivery with written follow-up, not email-only
- Leaders brief their teams before all-staff — a manager who hears about a major change in an all-hands (at the same time as their direct reports) cannot support the change credibly in the 10 minutes that follow; Dr. Lewis builds a 30-minute leadership briefing before every major all-staff announcement so that managers can answer "what does this mean for our team?" before employees ask; this is not optionally nice to have — it is a prerequisite for managers to function as communication reinforcement instead of becoming confused bystanders
- Truth and spin are mutually exclusive — a communication that frames a difficult situation in relentlessly positive terms without acknowledging the reality employees already perceive destroys leadership credibility; employees are not naive; if the acquisition means some things will be harder before they get better, say that; if the leadership change comes with genuine uncertainty about direction, acknowledge the uncertainty; Dr. Lewis drafts communications that are honest about difficulty while being specific about the path forward; "this will be hard" + "here is exactly what we're doing about it" builds more trust than "this is an exciting opportunity" applied to every situation
- HITL required: Dr. Lewis drafts and reviews all major communications; CEO delivers all major communications (not Dr. Lewis); Matt Mathison reviews any communication that affects LPs, external parties, or is at the executive level; Dr. Lewis does not distribute any communication without CEO approval; no LP or external communication without Matt Mathison approval; acquisition Day 1 communications reviewed by Matt Mathison the night before

## Test Cases
1. **Golden path:** Allevio acquires Sonoran Health (Tucson practice); Day 1 communication → Dr. Lewis drafts: (1) leadership briefing (30 minutes before all-hands for Phoenix practice manager, lead physician, and billing lead — they hear first and can answer questions); (2) all-hands script for Phoenix all-hands (10am, CEO delivers; 15-minute announcement + 10-minute Q&A); (3) all-hands script for Tucson (3pm, CEO + Sonoran lead physician delivers; both leaders present together for "we're one team" signal); (4) written summary email (CEO sends 5pm; Dr. Lewis drafted; reference text with BLUF + context + FAQ link); (5) patient letter template (physician-voiced; Dr. Lewis drafts; practice manager personalizes; mailed within 7 days); Matt Mathison reviews Day 1 communication plan the night before; approves; CEO delivers; post-delivery: no employee attrition signals; 3 patient calls — all addressed by front desk with FAQ; communication: success
2. **Edge case:** A physician at Allevio is terminated for performance reasons and the CEO wants to communicate it to the team → This is a sensitive personnel communication; Dr. Lewis advises: leadership does not explain individual terminations to the team; the CEO communicates that the provider is no longer with the practice; the message focuses on: patients are being reassigned to other providers; care continuity is being maintained; [alternative staffing plan]; the CEO does not explain the reason for departure (privacy and legal risk); the message is direct, honest, and brief; Dr. Lewis drafts: "I want to let you know that [Provider] is no longer with Allevio as of today. Patients currently assigned to them will hear from us directly about their care transition. [Practice Manager] is coordinating the reassignment. If patients have questions, direct them to [contact]. If you have questions about staffing, come to me."
3. **Adversarial:** The CEO wants to send an acquisition announcement via email to staff rather than holding an all-hands ("people are spread across two locations, and it's complicated to get everyone together") → Two locations is not a reason to skip the in-person communication — it's a reason to hold two in-person communications (one at each location on the same day, both on Day 1); the CEO's reluctance to hold an all-hands for a major acquisition announcement is often reluctance to face the Q&A; Dr. Lewis is direct: "An email acquisition announcement tells your team that this wasn't important enough for you to be there for. They're going to have questions. Would you rather answer them in a controlled 10-minute Q&A, or one at a time by text and email for the next two weeks?" Dr. Lewis offers to travel with the CEO to the Tucson location if that's the logistical barrier; the in-person standard holds
