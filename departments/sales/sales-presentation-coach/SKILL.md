# Sales Presentation Coach

Coach sales reps on presentation skills and executive communication for MBL portfolio companies — developing the rep's ability to deliver confident, credible presentations to C-suite decision-makers, navigate tough questions, and close the room. The best proposal in the world loses to a rep who can't hold the room for 30 minutes. Coaching is specific: don't say "be more confident" — say "pause 2 seconds before answering, make eye contact with the CFO, then answer."

## When to Use
- Preparing a rep for a first CFO or CEO meeting
- Reviewing a rep's presentation for an upcoming demo or QBR
- Coaching after a presentation that didn't convert
- Building a rep's long-term presentation capability

## Presentation Coaching Framework

```
COACHING PHILOSOPHY:
  Coaching is specific and behavioral — not generic ("be more confident")
  Coaching is forward-looking — "next time" behavior, not blame for the past
  Coaching uses the STAR format: Situation → Observation → Impact → Alternative
  
EXECUTIVE PRESENTATION PRINCIPLES:

  1. Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF — Dr. Lewis's standard):
     Lead with the conclusion, then support it
     Wrong: "I'm going to walk you through our solution, then our pricing, then our ROI model..."
     Right: "Here's the bottom line: employers in your situation typically see $180K in Year 1 savings.
             Here's how we get there."
     
  2. Lead with their problem, not your product:
     Wrong: "Allevio is a primary care platform that..."
     Right: "You told us your claims have gone up 12% for two years and your CFO wants it fixed.
             Here's exactly how Allevio solves that."
             
  3. Use numbers, not adjectives:
     Wrong: "We significantly reduce claims trends"
     Right: "We reduce claims trends from 8-15% down to 4-6% in Year 1 — measurable and guaranteed"
     
  4. Pause intentionally:
     After a data point: pause 2 seconds — let the number land
     Before answering a question: pause 2 seconds — don't rush
     A rep who talks continuously sounds nervous; a rep who pauses sounds confident
     
  5. Handle questions with the TBA framework:
     Think: pause before answering (never answer instantly)
     Bridge: "That's a great question — here's how I'd think about that..."
     Answer: give the answer; be direct
     
  6. Close the room:
     Every presentation ends with a next step proposed by the rep
     Don't wait for the executive to ask "so what happens now?"
     Rep owns the next step: "Based on what we've discussed, I'd like to propose [X]. Does [date] work?"
     
COMMON PRESENTATION FAILURES AND CORRECTIONS:

  Failure: Talking too fast when nervous
    Correction: "When you feel yourself speeding up, take a breath and drop your pace by half.
                 Practice the first 60 seconds of the presentation until you can deliver it slowly
                 without trying."
                 
  Failure: Starting with a feature tour ("Let me show you what we do...")
    Correction: "Start with their pain. The first 3 sentences should be about them, not you.
                 Start: 'You've described a 12% claims trend that your CFO is watching closely.
                 That's exactly what we're here to solve. Here's how.'"
                 
  Failure: Answering every question immediately without thinking
    Correction: "Pause before answering. Say: 'That's a good question. [pause] Here's how I'd
                 approach that.' The pause makes you look thoughtful, not slow."
                 
  Failure: Losing the room to side conversations
    Correction: "Direct address by name: 'Bill — does this align with what you're looking at from
                 a financial perspective?' Bring them back with a direct question."
                 
  Failure: Not proposing a next step
    Correction: "Every presentation ends with you proposing the next step — never asking 'so what
                 do you think?' Close: 'Based on what we've covered today, the logical next step is
                 [X]. I'll send a calendar invite for [specific date]. Does that work?'"
                 
  Failure: Hedging on the ROI ("It might be around 30%...")
    Correction: "Present the Conservative case as the floor. 'Our Conservative model, which uses
                 the lowest scenario we've seen, shows 30% reduction. Our base case is 40%.' 
                 Never present upside as the baseline."
                 
  Failure: Using internal jargon (PMPM; NRI; IVT) without explaining
    Correction: "Translate every term before using it: 'PMPM — that's the monthly per-employee
                 cost' — then use PMPM after that. If you're not sure if the term is familiar,
                 define it once."
                 
CFO-SPECIFIC COACHING:
  CFOs think in: ROI; payback period; risk; budget impact; year-over-year cost
  CFOs don't want: feature walkthroughs; stories about how great the product is
  CFO meeting structure:
    Minute 1: "The financial case for Allevio at [Company] is [X]." (Bottom line)
    Minutes 2-10: The cost-of-inaction analysis (what the trend costs at current trajectory)
    Minutes 10-20: The ROI model (Conservative; base; optimistic — always lead with Conservative)
    Minutes 20-25: Risk discussion (what guarantees exist; what happens if results underperform)
    Minute 25-30: Next step
    
  Questions to ask the CFO before presenting:
    "Before I run the model — what claims trend reduction would make this a clear yes for you?"
    "Are you comparing this against other cost-reduction initiatives in your budget?"
    "What's your timeline for making this decision?"
    
PRACTICE FRAMEWORK (before a high-stakes presentation):
  Step 1: Silent run (Rep alone — read through the full script)
  Step 2: Verbal run (Rep presents out loud; time it)
  Step 3: Rep with coach (Dr. Lewis or entity CEO as the CFO; rep runs the full 30 minutes)
  Step 4: Objection simulation (Coach asks the 5 hardest questions)
  Step 5: Next step practice (Rep closes the room; coach evaluates)
```

## Output Contract
- Coaching feedback is specific and behavioral, not evaluative — "you seemed nervous" is an evaluation; "you spoke 20% faster in the first 3 minutes than in the last 10, and you stopped making eye contact with the CFO when you got to the pricing section" is an observation the rep can act on; Dr. Lewis uses the STAR coaching format in every feedback session: Situation (what was happening), Observation (what I saw or heard), Impact (why it matters in the room), Alternative (what to do differently); the goal is one or two high-leverage behavioral changes per coaching session, not a comprehensive critique that overwhelms the rep
- Every coaching session produces a practice plan — coaching without a practice plan is feedback without accountability; after identifying the development areas, the coaching output is a specific practice schedule: when will they run through the presentation verbally? Who will be the "CFO" in the dry run? What are the 5 hardest questions the coach will throw? The practice plan is logged in GHL so the entity CEO knows which reps are being coached and for which presentations; if a rep has a CFO meeting next week and there's no dry run scheduled, Dr. Lewis escalates — the dry run is not optional for presentations >$100K
- HITL required: all Allevio case studies or ROI data used in coached presentations → HIPAA-compliant (employer-aggregate only; no PHI); entity CEO notified if coaching reveals a pattern of HIPAA or pricing-authority issues (rep consistently referencing individual employee health; rep consistently making concessions without authorization); >$100K CFO presentation → entity CEO briefed before the meeting; coaching report filed in SharePoint → entity CEO has visibility into rep development; coaching for HIVE seller meetings → Dr. Lewis or Matt Mathison leads (not a rep solo); all HIVE seller presentations → Matt Mathison approves content before delivery

## Test Cases
1. **Golden path:** Rep has a CFO meeting for a 200-employee self-insured Allevio employer — $180K ACV at stake. Coaching session 5 days before the meeting. Dry run — Rep presents the full 30 minutes. Observations: (1) Opening: Rep said "Let me show you what Allevio does" → correction: "Lead with their pain. 'You've described a 12% claims trend your CFO is watching closely. That's what we're here to solve.' Try it." (2) ROI model: Rep said "we think we can usually get around 30-40% reduction" → correction: "Present Conservative as the floor: 'Our Conservative case — the lowest we've seen — is 30%. Our base case is 40%. Here's the model at 30% for your employee count.'" (3) Closing: Rep said "So what do you think?" → correction: "Propose the next step: 'Based on what we've covered, I'd like to get your legal team and ours on a 30-minute call to answer any contract questions. Does [date] work?'" Practice plan: verbal run tomorrow (timed; solo); second dry run with entity CEO as CFO (2 days before meeting). Entity CEO notified. Green light after second dry run.
2. **Edge case:** Rep is presenting to an HR Director who asked to bring their benefits broker to the meeting without telling the rep in advance. Rep calls Dr. Lewis 30 minutes before: "The broker is in the room — I wasn't expecting this." Quick coaching: "Brokers can be champions or obstacles — find out which. In the first 5 minutes: 'Thank you for joining us today — what's your primary interest in seeing what Allevio offers?' If the broker is a current Allevio broker: great; engage them as a team member. If they're from a competing platform: acknowledge them professionally; never disparage their platform; focus on value that differentiates Allevio on the employer's stated pain. If the broker has influence over the decision: 'I'd love to follow up with you separately to give you everything you need to evaluate Allevio for your book of business.' Never go around the broker in the room."
3. **Adversarial:** Rep had a CFO meeting last week that didn't go well — "The CFO basically killed it after 10 minutes." Coaching investigation: What happened in the first 10 minutes? "I walked through the platform — feature by feature — and the CFO interrupted and said 'I don't really care about the features; what does this actually cost me and what do I get?'" Dr. Lewis: "The CFO gave you the perfect coaching moment. A CFO who asks 'what does this cost and what do I get?' is telling you they were not hearing a business case; they were hearing a product tour. The correction: BLUF from Minute 1. The first sentence to a CFO is the bottom line: 'At your employee count, the Conservative case for Allevio saves you $X in Year 1. Here's how.' Then you earn the features by connecting each one to the ROI. The CFO will ask you questions about features if they believe in the ROI — but they'll never believe in the ROI if they don't hear it first. Let's do a full dry run this week. I'll be the CFO and I'll push back at every point. The goal is that you don't make it past Minute 1 without the bottom line."
