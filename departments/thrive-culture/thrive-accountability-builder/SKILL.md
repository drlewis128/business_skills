# THRIVE Accountability Builder

Accountability at MBL means owning the outcome — not just the task, not just the effort, and not just the part that went well. This skill helps entity CEOs and managers build a culture where commitments are made intentionally, followed through with ownership, and reviewed with honesty — aligned with THRIVE's Hustle and Integrity pillars.

## When to Use

- A team is confusing effort with outcome, activity with accountability, or explanation with ownership
- An ops review reveals a pattern of accountability-avoidance: blaming circumstances, systems, or other teams for missed outcomes
- A new entity CEO or manager needs to understand and model MBL's accountability standard from day one
- Dr. Lewis or Callie is building accountability content for the THRIVE playbook

## Accountability Builder Framework

```
THRIVE ACCOUNTABILITY BUILDER FRAMEWORK
=========================================

SECTION 1 — ACCOUNTABILITY DEFINED (MBL OPERATING STANDARD)
  Accountability = I own the outcome, not just the task
  Task ownership: I did what I was assigned
  Outcome ownership: I ensured the result was achieved — including managing dependencies, surfacing risks, and escalating when needed
  Accountability is not self-punishment for failure — it is honest ownership of what happened and clear ownership of what comes next
  Blame is backward-looking; accountability is forward-looking

SECTION 2 — ACCOUNTABILITY VS. BLAME
  Blame: assigns fault to a person or circumstance; closes the conversation
  Accountability: assigns ownership for the next action; opens the path forward
  Accountability language: "I missed this. Here's what happened. Here's what I'll do differently."
  Blame language: "This didn't happen because of [person / system / circumstance]."
  Both can be true simultaneously — accountability does not erase cause, but cause does not replace accountability
  Managers who model blame culture produce blame cultures; managers who model ownership produce ownership cultures

SECTION 3 — COMMITMENT-MAKING PROTOCOL
  Do not say yes unless you mean yes
  Before committing: "Do I have the time, resources, and clarity to deliver this on this date?"
  If no: negotiate scope, timeline, or resources — do not commit and then miss
  Saying no is an Integrity act when you know you cannot deliver
  Weak commitments ("I'll try," "I'll see what I can do") = not commitments; ask for a yes or no
  Matt Mathison standard: "Yes" means yes. "Maybe" means no. "I'll try" means no.

SECTION 4 — WHEN TO SAY NO
  Say no when: you lack the information to commit, the timeline is not achievable, or the dependency is uncontrolled
  How to say no: "I can't commit to that by [date] because [specific reason]. I can commit to [alternative]."
  Saying no is not a Hustle failure — over-committing and missing is a Hustle + Integrity failure
  Entity CEO permission to say no to Matt Mathison when the commitment is not achievable
  Pushback from leadership does not change the facts — it changes the pressure; acknowledge both

SECTION 5 — ACCOUNTABILITY IN REVIEWS
  Every ops review: "What did we commit to? What did we deliver? What do we own for the gap?"
  Acceptable review response for a miss: "[item] was not delivered. [Root cause]. [What I own to close it]. New commitment: [date]."
  Not acceptable: "[item] was not delivered because [list of external factors]" — without "and here's what I own"
  Pattern of missed commitments (≥2 in a row): triggers 1:1 accountability coaching, not just re-commitment
  Entity CEO accountability reviews: Dr. Lewis reviews entity CEO delivery against stated commitments quarterly

SECTION 6 — MATT MATHISON'S ACCOUNTABILITY STANDARD
  Matt Mathison holds himself accountable first — models before expecting
  He expects entity CEOs to own their entity outcomes fully — not explain them
  The format he expects in updates: [What I said I'd do] → [What happened] → [What I now own]
  He interprets external blame without ownership as a signal the leader is not ready for more scope
  His response to honest accountability for a miss: "Good. What's the plan?" — not punishment

SECTION 7 — ACCOUNTABILITY METRICS
  Commitment-keeping rate: ≥95% target (all entities)
  Escalation-before-miss rate: % of misses where the person raised the flag before the deadline
  Re-commitment accuracy: when a new date is set after a miss, is it kept? (Target: 100%)
  Self-accountability signal: does the person identify the gap before being asked? (qualitative)
  Entity CEO accountability: quarterly review by Dr. Lewis; annual review with Matt Mathison

SECTION 8 — ENTITY-SPECIFIC ACCOUNTABILITY CONTEXT
  MBL HoldCo:   Accountability for portfolio strategy execution and capital deployment decisions
  Allevio:       Accountability for care quality, member outcomes, and billing accuracy
  HIVE:          Accountability for production targets, LP returns, and field safety outcomes
  Column6:       Accountability for client campaign results and gross margin maintenance
```

## Output Contract

- **Accountability is measured on outcomes, not intentions, because intentions are not auditable.** A team member who consistently misses commitments with excellent explanations is not accountable — they are articulate. MBL's standard requires that the outcome is owned, not just explained. The metrics (commitment-keeping rate, escalation-before-miss, re-commitment accuracy) are designed to make accountability observable and comparable over time, not a judgment call each review cycle.

- **The commitment-making protocol is enforced because weak commitments are the origin of accountability culture failure.** When people say "I'll try" and miss, the miss is attributed to circumstances rather than to the fact that a real commitment was never made. By requiring that "yes" means yes and "I'll try" means no, the skill removes the ambiguity that allows people to miss without owning a miss. This is a language intervention with structural consequences.

- **Entity CEO accountability is reviewed by Dr. Lewis quarterly because entity CEOs have the fewest people who will hold them accountable.** The power dynamic that makes entity CEOs effective also insulates them from the feedback and accountability that others receive. The quarterly Dr. Lewis review is the structural mechanism that ensures the entity CEO's commitments to Matt Mathison are tracked, their ownership language is assessed, and their accountability modeling is evaluated — because the culture of the entire entity follows the CEO's example.

## Test Cases

1. **Golden path:** Allevio ops review shows care coordination commitment-keeping rate at 91%, with three repeat misses by the same care coordinator. Skill generates an accountability assessment, identifies no escalation-before-miss behavior in any of the three cases, and produces a coaching plan with specific commitment-making protocol training and a 30-day re-assessment.

2. **Edge case:** A HIVE production manager misses a target because of a genuine equipment failure (force majeure) but still presents the outcome with accountability language: "Production was 92% of target. The pump failure cost us 3 BOE/day for 4 days. I own the recovery plan and here's the new timeline." Skill scores this as strong accountability despite the miss, notes the distinction between cause and ownership, and positively flags the model behavior for entity CEO recognition.

3. **Adversarial:** A Column6 manager submits ops updates that always include external factors as the primary explanation for misses but never use ownership language. Skill detects the pattern (5 consecutive updates with blame-first language, 0 with ownership language), flags it as a WEAK accountability signal, and routes to entity CEO with a conversation prep guide — noting that the pattern has been consistent for 5 weeks and now rises above individual coaching to culture-level intervention.
