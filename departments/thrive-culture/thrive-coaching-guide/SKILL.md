# THRIVE Coaching Guide

Coaching is how MBL develops people through the THRIVE lens before they need a PIP. This skill equips managers, entity CEOs, and Dr. Lewis with a structured coaching approach that strengthens specific THRIVE pillars — using the right questions, the right frequency, and a clear distinction between coaching conversations and performance management conversations.

## When to Use

- A manager wants to develop a team member on a specific THRIVE dimension without triggering a formal performance process
- An entity CEO needs a structured 1:1 coaching framework for their direct reports
- Dr. Lewis is coaching an entity CEO on a specific THRIVE leadership gap
- Callie is building the coaching content for the THRIVE manager playbook

## Coaching Guide Framework

```
THRIVE COACHING GUIDE FRAMEWORK
==================================

SECTION 1 — COACHING VS. MANAGING DISTINCTION
  Managing:  Setting expectations, tracking delivery, holding accountability — necessary; not coaching
  Coaching:  Developing capability and insight so the person performs better without being told to
  Managing without coaching = compliance culture; coaching without accountability = nice but ineffective
  The right sequence: coach first; manage when coaching has been applied and behavior hasn't changed
  Coaching is an investment in the person's future performance; managing addresses current performance

SECTION 2 — WHEN COACHING IS APPROPRIATE VS. PIP
  COACH when: the gap is a capability or insight gap; the person is willing and self-aware; they have not yet had a fair chance to succeed with development support
  PIP when: expectations have been clearly communicated, coaching has occurred, behavior has not changed despite support, and the gap is now a performance failure, not a development opportunity
  Do not skip coaching and go straight to PIP — it is unfair and legally fragile
  Do not use coaching to avoid a PIP that is already warranted — it delays the inevitable and punishes the team
  Integrity violations: do not coach — they go directly to the Integrity conversation protocol (see thrive-difficult-conversation-guide)

SECTION 3 — 1:1 COACHING AGENDA (THRIVE FORMAT)
  Recommended agenda (45-60 minutes):
    1. OPEN (5 min): "What's on your mind? Anything urgent before we dive in?"
    2. TRUTH CHECK (10 min): "What's going well, really? What's not? What are you not saying that you should?"
    3. HUSTLE PROGRESS (10 min): "What commitments did you make since we last met? What did you deliver?"
    4. THRIVE PILLAR FOCUS (20 min): [coach on 1 pillar specific to development area — see Section 5]
    5. CLOSE (5 min): "What are you taking away from today? What will you do differently before we meet again?"
  Document: date, pillar focus, key insight from the person (not just what the coach said), commitment made
  Frequency: weekly for active development; biweekly for maintenance coaching

SECTION 4 — COACHING PRINCIPLES (THRIVE ALIGNED)
  Ask more than tell: coaching questions develop insight; telling develops compliance
  Truth first: open the coaching space by naming what is real — including your own observations
  Respect the person's agency: coaching produces the person's own answer — not your answer
  Hustle in coaching: don't let the conversation go in circles; move toward a commitment and close
  Integrity in coaching: if you say you'll follow up, follow up — broken coaching commitments destroy trust
  Value: every coaching session ends with at least one behavioral commitment — otherwise it was a conversation

SECTION 5 — COACHING BY THRIVE PILLAR
  COACHING TRUTH:
    Core question: "Where are you holding back the full truth in your work? What would happen if you said it?"
    Development focus: creating the habit of proactive disclosure; reducing the habit of softening or omitting
    Practice: assign one "truth I've been holding back" to surface before next session

  COACHING HUSTLE:
    Core question: "What did you commit to? What did you deliver? What got in the way? What will you do differently?"
    Development focus: commitment accuracy, escalation-before-miss, self-initiation
    Practice: commitment audit — the person reviews their own commitments from the prior period and self-scores

  COACHING RESPECT:
    Core question: "How did you show up in your most challenging relationship this week? What would you do differently?"
    Development focus: inclusive language, debating ideas not people, acknowledging contribution
    Practice: one specific respectful behavior change in their highest-friction relationship before next session

  COACHING INTEGRITY:
    Core question: "Is there anything you said you'd do that you haven't done? What are you going to do about it?"
    Development focus: commitment accuracy, proactive disclosure of gaps, self-accountability
    Practice: the person identifies one commitment they've been avoiding closing — and closes it

  COACHING VALUE:
    Core question: "What is the most valuable thing you could do with your next 10 hours? Is that what you're doing?"
    Development focus: prioritization, output-over-activity orientation, ROI thinking applied to time
    Practice: 80/20 time audit — the person lists their top activities and estimates value-per-hour

  COACHING ENJOY:
    Core question: "When was the last time you felt genuinely energized by your work here? What created that?"
    Development focus: identifying and protecting high-engagement activities; reducing disengagement drivers
    Practice: the person redesigns one aspect of their role to increase their enjoyment and bring a proposal next session

SECTION 6 — MANAGER SELF-COACHING (LEADERS COACH THEMSELVES FIRST)
  Leaders who do not coach themselves cannot coach others credibly
  Manager self-coaching cadence: monthly — 30 minutes of private THRIVE pillar self-reflection
  Self-coaching questions:
    "Which pillar did I model best this month? Which did I let slide?"
    "What is the hardest truth I need to tell right now that I've been avoiding?"
    "Is my team energized because of how I show up, or despite it?"
  Self-coaching is not self-flagellation — it is honest self-assessment with forward commitment

SECTION 7 — COACHING ROLES AT MBL
  Callie:       Coordinates THRIVE playbook skill development; coaches managers on THRIVE coaching tools; works with Fitz on skills testing
  Dr. Lewis:    Coaches entity CEOs on leadership dimensions; coordinates coaching with entity CEO development plans
  Entity CEOs:  Coach their direct report managers using this framework
  Managers:     Coach individual contributors using this framework
  Matt Mathison: Does not conduct formal coaching sessions — his coaching is embedded in every interaction; entity CEOs treat every interaction with Matt as a coaching signal
```

## Output Contract

- **The coaching session ends with a behavioral commitment because insight without commitment produces learning, not change.** A person who leaves a coaching session feeling good but with no specific action to take before the next session has had a pleasant conversation — not a coaching experience. The behavioral commitment is the mechanism that converts insight into behavior change, and it is the item that opens the next session's accountability check. The skill enforces the commitment requirement because without it, coaching is indistinguishable from therapy.

- **Coaching by THRIVE pillar is more effective than generic coaching because it gives both the coach and the coachee a shared vocabulary and a clear developmental focus.** When a manager tells someone "you need to communicate better," the feedback is too vague to act on. When they say "I want to coach you on your Truth pillar — specifically, how you communicate project status to leadership before a miss," the development focus is precise and the coachee can connect their behavior to a value they understand. The pillar framework is the shared language that makes coaching precise.

- **Manager self-coaching is required before coaching others because leaders who skip self-reflection become advice-dispensing machines, not coaches.** A manager who coaches their team on Truth but avoids hard truths in their own upward communication creates a credibility gap that the team will detect. The monthly self-coaching requirement is not performative — it is the mechanism that keeps the coaching relationship honest and ensures the manager is developing alongside the people they are coaching.

## Test Cases

1. **Golden path:** A Column6 account manager is being coached on the Hustle pillar — specifically, on escalating before a miss rather than after. Session 3 of 4 in the development cycle. The coachee self-identifies that they delay escalation because they fear being seen as unable to handle the situation. Skill generates a session summary with that insight quoted, a specific behavioral commitment (escalate the next at-risk delivery within 24 hours of identifying the risk), and a Hustle coaching note for the entity CEO on progress trajectory.

2. **Edge case:** A HIVE field manager is resistant to the 1:1 coaching format, finding it too "formal" for the field culture. Skill adapts the format: shorter (20-minute) check-in framing; replaces THRIVE language with field-native language (e.g., "do what you say, hit your numbers, respect the crew"); maintains all structural elements (truth check, commitment, follow-up) in a format the manager can own. The pillar focus is Hustle, applied to production commitment accuracy.

3. **Adversarial:** A manager uses the coaching session notes as evidence in a subsequent disciplinary conversation, quoting the coachee's own admissions against them. Skill flags this as a coaching process violation: session notes are development documents, not disciplinary evidence. The skill recommends that disciplinary conversations use separate documentation drawn from performance observations, not coaching notes. Dr. Lewis is notified; the manager is coached on the distinction between coaching and managing documentation.

## Audit Log

All coaching session summaries, behavioral commitments, pillar focus history, and progress notes are logged with date, entity, coach identity, coachee role, and pillar. Coaching records are maintained for 24 months and referenced in performance reviews and promotion decisions. Coaching records are not shared with parties outside the coach-coachee-entity CEO chain without the coachee's consent, except in PIP or legal review contexts. Dr. Lewis reviews entity CEO coaching logs quarterly.
