# THRIVE Conflict Resolver

Conflict is not a THRIVE violation — avoidance is. This skill guides managers and entity CEOs through MBL's conflict resolution protocol, applying the Respect + Truth pillars in combination to distinguish productive disagreement from values violations, and to escalate appropriately when conflict crosses from operational to ethical.

## When to Use

- Two or more team members are in conflict that is affecting team performance, morale, or communication
- A manager is unsure whether a conflict requires HR involvement, entity CEO involvement, or can be resolved at the team level
- A conflict pattern has emerged (recurring friction between the same individuals or functions) that signals a deeper values or structural misalignment
- A conflict has escalated to the point where separation may be the only resolution

## Conflict Resolver Framework

```
THRIVE CONFLICT RESOLVER FRAMEWORK
=====================================

SECTION 1 — CONFLICT TYPE CLASSIFICATION
  INTERPERSONAL:  Friction between individuals driven by style, communication, or relationship
  VALUES:         Disagreement rooted in different beliefs about how work should be done or what matters
  RESOURCE:       Competition for budget, headcount, attention, or credit
  STRATEGIC:      Disagreement about direction, priority, or decision-making authority
  THRIVE VIOLATION: Any conflict type where a THRIVE pillar is actively being breached (see Section 5)

SECTION 2 — THRIVE CONFLICT RESOLUTION PROTOCOL
  Step 1 — CLASSIFY: Identify conflict type; determine THRIVE pillars implicated
  Step 2 — ISOLATE: Separate the parties; no unmediated joint confrontation before preparation
  Step 3 — LISTEN: Manager or entity CEO hears each party separately (Truth dimension)
  Step 4 — FIND FACT: What specifically happened? What was the impact? Behavioral language only
  Step 5 — MEDIATE: Bring parties together with a structured agenda (see Section 3)
  Step 6 — RESOLVE: Define the expected behavior change from each party with a timeline
  Step 7 — DOCUMENT: Write up the resolution, behavioral commitments, and follow-up date
  Step 8 — FOLLOW UP: Check in with both parties at 2 weeks and 30 days

SECTION 3 — MANAGER FACILITATION GUIDE (MEDIATED SESSION)
  Agenda: 1) ground rules (Respect protocol), 2) each party states the impact (not intent), 3) facilitator synthesizes, 4) each party proposes one change they will make, 5) agreement + timeline
  Facilitator role: neutral; not judge; not therapist; not the party who decides who is right
  Prohibited: relitigating history beyond what establishes the current impact; character attacks
  Outcome required: specific behavioral commitments from each party — not just "we'll try to get along"

SECTION 4 — RESOLUTION PRINCIPLES (THRIVE LENS)
  Truth:     Both parties state impact honestly; facilitator does not smooth over real issues
  Respect:   Process treats both parties with equal dignity regardless of seniority
  Integrity: Commitments made in the resolution session are binding — not suggestions
  Hustle:    Resolution moves to closure efficiently; no open-ended "let's see how it goes"
  Value:     Resolution restores the team's ability to create value — that is the standard of success
  Enjoy:     A team in sustained conflict cannot enjoy its work — resolution restores that floor

SECTION 5 — WHEN CONFLICT BECOMES A THRIVE VIOLATION
  Conflict becomes a THRIVE violation when:
    - One party is deliberately harassing, demeaning, or retaliating against the other (Respect + Integrity)
    - One party is withholding information, lying, or misrepresenting facts (Integrity + Truth)
    - One party is sabotaging the other's work (Integrity + Hustle)
    - The conflict is driven by discrimination, bias, or protected-class targeting (legal + THRIVE)
  THRIVE violation classification: escalates immediately to HR + entity CEO
  Do NOT attempt standard conflict resolution when a THRIVE violation is confirmed

SECTION 6 — ENTITY CEO INVOLVEMENT
  Entity CEO joins conflict resolution when: conflict involves a manager-level role, the conflict is visible to the broader team, or the conflict has persisted beyond one mediation attempt
  Entity CEO leads resolution when: conflict involves two direct reports, or the conflict is values-level
  Entity CEO escalates to Dr. Lewis when: the conflict involves themselves as a party, or the resolution involves a potential separation decision

SECTION 7 — SEPARATION AS RESOLUTION
  Separation is the appropriate resolution when: repeated mediation has produced no durable change, the conflict is a confirmed THRIVE violation, or the cost to the team of continued conflict exceeds the cost of losing one party
  Separation decision: entity CEO + Dr. Lewis; Matt Mathison notified same day
  When both parties are at fault: the party whose behavior breaches a THRIVE pillar is separated first; resource disputes that have no clear THRIVE violation may result in role restructuring instead
```

## Output Contract

- **Conflict type classification determines the resolution path, and mixing paths produces worse outcomes.** A values conflict treated as an interpersonal conflict gets facilitated into a behavioral agreement that never addresses the underlying disagreement about how work should be done — so the conflict resurfaces within weeks. A THRIVE violation treated as a resource conflict skips the HR escalation that the violation requires and exposes the organization legally. The skill enforces type classification first because the wrong path wastes time and increases risk.

- **Behavioral commitments in the resolution session are binding, not aspirational.** The Integrity pillar requires that commitments made in a formal resolution process are honored — because if they are not, the violating party has now demonstrated that they will not honor agreements when accountable others are watching. A broken resolution commitment triggers an immediate escalation to entity CEO and is documented as an Integrity signal in the party's file.

- **Separation as a resolution option is stated explicitly because avoiding it often causes more harm than the separation itself.** Organizations that keep parties in unresolvable conflict damage the entire team: the people who witness the ongoing conflict lose faith in leadership, the conflicting parties suffer, and the team's output degrades. The skill surfaces separation as a legitimate and sometimes necessary resolution so entity CEOs do not default to indefinite mediation when the evidence points to an irreconcilable THRIVE violation.

## Test Cases

1. **Golden path:** Two Allevio care coordinators are in resource conflict over patient assignment authority, creating delays in care coordination. Manager facilitates a structured mediation session, each party commits to a specific handoff protocol, and the resolution is documented with a 2-week follow-up. No THRIVE violation; resolved at manager level.

2. **Edge case:** A conflict at HIVE between a field crew lead and an office-based operations manager involves a genuine strategic disagreement about production protocols — not interpersonal friction. Standard mediation produces no resolution because neither party is wrong on the merits. Skill reclassifies as a strategic conflict, routes to entity CEO for a decision (not mediation), and documents the decision so both parties have a clear path forward.

3. **Adversarial:** A manager attempts to use the conflict resolution process as a performance management tool — framing a PIP-eligible subordinate as the "other party in a conflict" to avoid the formal PIP process. Skill identifies the classification mismatch (one party has no active conflict behavior; the other is a manager initiating the session), flags it as a potential process misuse, and routes to entity CEO and HR for reclassification as a performance management issue.

## Audit Log

All conflict classifications, mediation session summaries, resolution commitments, follow-up check-ins, and escalation decisions are logged with entity, date, parties (by role at reporting level), conflict type, THRIVE pillars implicated, and outcome. THRIVE violation conflicts are flagged in a high-priority HR audit trail. Logs are retained for 36 months given the legal and HR relevance of interpersonal and values conflicts.
