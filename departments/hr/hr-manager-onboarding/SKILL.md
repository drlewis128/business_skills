# HR Manager Onboarding

Onboard and prepare a new or first-time manager at an MBL portfolio company — equipping them with the fundamentals of managing people before their first management failures create trust damage or team turnover. The transition from individual contributor to manager is one of the hardest career transitions. Skills that made them excellent as an individual contributor (doing excellent work, moving fast, knowing the right answer) are often counterproductive as a manager (they need to delegate, slow down to develop others, coach rather than tell). This plan structures that transition.

## When to Use
- An employee is being promoted into their first management role
- A new manager has joined from outside the company (external hire)
- An existing manager is taking on a larger team or a different function for the first time
- A manager is struggling early and needs structured support
- Matt Mathison asks "how are we preparing [Name] for the manager role?"

## New Manager Onboarding Framework

```
Two scenarios:

INTERNAL PROMOTION TO MANAGER:
  Challenge: Former peer is now their manager. Team may have mixed feelings.
  Priority: Establish new role clarity quickly; avoid reverting to peer behavior
  Key support: Dr. Lewis coaches through the "former peer" dynamic in Week 1

EXTERNAL HIRE AS MANAGER:
  Challenge: Needs to understand the team's work before managing it
  Priority: Learn before leading — listen in the first 30 days; avoid early major changes
  Key support: Introduction framing; team history; prior manager context

Four core manager fundamentals (every new manager, every time):

1. SET EXPECTATIONS: Do my direct reports know what they're supposed to do and why?
   → Clear role descriptions, goals, and performance standards for each report

2. GIVE FEEDBACK: Am I providing specific, timely, honest feedback?
   → Regular 1:1s with feedback agenda
   → Positive feedback for specific behaviors (not just "good job")
   → Improvement feedback delivered within 48 hours of observation, not at review

3. DEVELOP PEOPLE: Am I investing in each person's growth?
   → One development conversation per person per quarter minimum

4. PROTECT THE TEAM: Am I removing obstacles and advocating for my team?
   → Operational blockers removed; escalations made; credit given where due
```

## Month 1: Learn Before Leading

**Priority:** Observe, ask questions, and understand before making changes.

| Practice | Frequency | Notes |
|---------|----------|-------|
| Weekly 1:1 with each direct report (30 min) | Weekly | Use a consistent agenda; listen first |

| Skill | Target | How |

## Manager Fundamentals Reference

### 1:1 Meeting Agenda Template (30 min, weekly)

| Time | Topic |
|------|-------|
| 0-5 min | "What's on your mind?" — employee drives; manager listens |
| 5-20 min | Work updates — progress on goals; blockers; what they need |
| 20-28 min | Manager feedback — specific positive + one improvement observation |
| 28-30 min | Next steps — what are they committing to before next week? |

### Feedback Formula

Good feedback: "[Specific behavior] → [Specific impact] → [What I'd like to see instead / keep doing]"

Example (improvement): "In yesterday's call, you described the timeline to the client as 'probably 3-4 weeks' when we both know it's more likely 6-8 weeks. That sets us up for a difficult conversation in a month. Next time, let's agree on the answer before client calls."

Example (positive): "You flagged the AP overdue situation before I knew about it — and you already had a plan. That's exactly the proactive ownership I'm looking for."

### How to Handle Team Members Who Push Back on the New Manager

"If someone challenges your authority or says 'that's not how we did it before,' the answer is: 'I hear you — let's talk about the reason behind the change. I'm not changing things for the sake of it, but I do want to understand both the old way and your perspective before we decide together.' You have the authority; you don't need to exercise it by force."

## Output Contract
- Week 1 focus is listening, not changing — the most common mistake of a new manager (especially an internal promotion) is to start making changes before understanding why things are the way they are; the first-week plan explicitly restricts the new manager to listening and learning; Dr. Lewis reinforces this in the Day 1 conversation
- Internal promotion dynamics handled explicitly — the "former peer is now my manager" dynamic is the most common failure mode in internal promotions; it is addressed in Week 1 with a team conversation and explicit coaching on how to navigate the shift without either reverting to peer behavior or overcorrecting with authoritarian management
- Feedback delivery coached before expected — the 4 fundamentals include feedback, but feedback is a skill most new managers lack; Dr. Lewis coaches the specific feedback formula and the "within 48 hours" rule before the manager is expected to deliver their first improvement feedback; coaching before the attempt prevents the common pattern of feedback avoidance ("they're doing fine" when they're actually not)
- Week 12 effectiveness check cross-referenced with team — the manager's self-assessment at Week 12 is valuable; the brief pulse with 1-2 direct reports gives a second data point; if the manager says "I'm doing great" and the team says "we never know what's expected of us," there's a coaching opportunity that the self-assessment alone would miss
- HITL required: Dr. Lewis coaches all new managers; coaching check-ins at Week 2, 4, 8, 12; team pulse at Week 12 with direct reports (confidential); any early warning sign (team member expressing concern, significant performance decline in the team) escalated to CEO and Matt Mathison immediately; new manager struggling pattern reported to Matt Mathison at Week 4 if not improving

## System Dependencies
- **Reads from:** Direct report role descriptions and current goals, prior manager context, team operating rhythm, new manager's own performance review and development history
- **Writes to:** Manager onboarding plan (SharePoint/HR/<Company>/Leadership/); Week 12 assessment; Matt Mathison brief (if concern identified); direct report pulse notes (confidential)
- **HITL Required:** Dr. Lewis coaches and facilitates; early warning signs escalated immediately; Week 12 cross-reference with direct reports; Matt Mathison aware of all new manager transitions

## Test Cases
1. **Golden path:** Allevio senior coordinator promoted to practice manager (3 direct reports) → Week 1: Dr. Lewis Day 1 conversation covers 4 fundamentals, the "former peer" dynamic (she was peers with 2 of her 3 reports), and the first-week listening plan; team meeting: CEO acknowledges the promotion to the team, explains the specific reason ("Maria's operational excellence and her ability to develop the coordinators around her"), sets expectations; Days 2-4: Maria does 30-min 1:1s with each report — learns that one report is worried about the role change, one is enthusiastic, one is neutral; Day 5 check-in: Dr. Lewis coaches on the anxious report: "Have a direct conversation with her — not about whether she wanted the job, but about what she needs from you to feel set up for success"; Month 1: weekly 1:1s with consistent agenda; "State of the Team" delivered on Day 30 — thoughtful, specific; Month 2: goals set with each report; first improvement feedback delivered (with Dr. Lewis coaching beforehand); Week 12 assessment: Maria rates herself 4/5 confidence; direct report pulse: consistent positive signal with one note on "sometimes jumps to solutions before fully hearing the question" — coaching topic for next check-in
2. **Edge case:** At Week 4, one direct report has approached Dr. Lewis directly and said "I don't think [new manager] knows what they're doing and the team is frustrated" → this is not ignored; Dr. Lewis has a confidential conversation to understand the specific concern; if the concern is about a skill gap (new manager is avoiding feedback conversations), this is addressed with the manager directly in the Week 4 coaching call with specific examples; if the concern is about a conduct issue (new manager is showing favoritism, taking credit for team work), this is escalated to the CEO and Matt Mathison; the direct report's confidence is maintained throughout
3. **Adversarial:** A CEO says "just throw them in the deep end — that's how I learned to manage" → present the data: "Some managers thrive in that environment; others create team turnover and performance problems while figuring it out. At our current team size, one manager who takes 12 months to find their footing may cost us 2-3 good employees who couldn't work through the uncertainty. The 12-week plan is mostly Dr. Lewis coaching time — the direct cost is minimal. The indirect cost of not doing it is potentially significant. Can we try the plan for the first 8 weeks and you can evaluate whether it's adding value?"

## Audit Log
New manager onboarding plans retained by manager and date. Coaching check-in notes retained. Week 12 self-assessment retained. Team pulse notes retained confidentially. Early warning escalations documented. Matt Mathison notifications retained.

## Deprecation
Retire when portfolio companies have dedicated L&D functions with manager development programs, trained HR partners who conduct manager onboarding coaching, and established management competency frameworks that don't require Dr. Lewis's direct coaching for each new manager transition.
