# PE Exit Value Optimizer

Maximize exit value for MBL Partners portfolio company sales — developing the buyer value narrative, identifying which buyer segments pay the highest multiples, structuring the exit to create competitive tension, and positioning the business to command a premium over the baseline fair market value. Exit value optimization is not spin — it is the disciplined work of understanding what buyers in each segment are willing to pay and why, then presenting the business in a way that makes the highest value buyers the most motivated; a healthcare MSO that runs a broadly distributed CIM gets healthcare-generic multiples; a healthcare MSO that runs a targeted process for employer-sponsored health plan acquirers who specifically need claims management infrastructure gets a strategic premium.

## When to Use
- Exit preparation: building the value narrative for the CIM
- Buyer targeting: identifying which buyer segments pay the most
- Process design: creating competitive tension to maximize price
- Matt Mathison asks "how do we get the best price?"

## Exit Value Optimization Framework

```
VALUE DRIVER ANALYSIS (what buyers pay for):

  FINANCIAL VALUE DRIVERS:
    EBITDA quality: recurring; contract-based; defensible (high quality = higher multiple)
    Revenue growth trajectory: buyers pay for future; growing EBITDA gets higher multiples
    Margin profile: high margin businesses command premium multiples vs. low margin
    Working capital efficiency: low WC requirement = lower capital needs = higher equity value
    
  STRATEGIC VALUE DRIVERS (unique to each entity):
    Scale: bigger businesses get higher multiples (size premium for buyers)
    Platform: is this company a platform for a buy-and-build roll-up? (PE pays for platforms)
    Technology moats: proprietary technology that creates defensible market position
    Data moats: proprietary data that a strategic buyer needs and cannot replicate
    Geographic expansion: ability to replicate the model in new markets post-acquisition
    Customer relationships: long-tenure; sticky; diversified customer base
    
  ENTITY-SPECIFIC VALUE DRIVERS:
  
    ALLEVIO:
      Strategic premium drivers: employer-sponsored care management infrastructure
      (larger health plan operators or MSOs need operational infrastructure they don't have)
      Data premium: claims data and employer group relationships (actuarial value to payers)
      Platform premium: MBL has proven the MSO model can be replicated → platform PE buyer
      Geographic expansion: Allevio model can be replicated in new markets post-acquisition
      Key buyers who pay strategic premiums: large regional health plans; national MSO platforms;
      PE firms with healthcare roll-up thesis
      HIPAA: all buyer-facing value narratives use aggregate data — no individual PHI
      
    HIVE:
      Strategic premium drivers: Uinta Basin royalty positions with development upside
      Landowner relationship premium: long-tenure royalty owner relationships reduce re-signing risk
      Acreage premium: specific formation access (operator buyers need specific acreage)
      Key buyers who pay strategic premiums: active Uinta Basin E&P operators;
      royalty aggregators building Uinta exposure; PE firms with royalty acquisition thesis
      
    COLUMN6:
      Strategic premium drivers: CTV inventory access + agency relationships
      VCR/IVT performance premium: verified clean inventory at scale
      Agency roster premium: long-tenure agency relationships
      Key buyers who pay strategic premiums: holding company media networks;
      streaming platform ad sales divisions; PE roll-ups in CTV/programmatic

AUCTION PROCESS DYNAMICS (how to create competitive tension):

  CREATING BUYER COMPETITION:
    Run a broad first round → selective second round → tight final round
    Broad first round: 30-50 teasers → 15-25 NDAs → 8-15 IOIs
    Selective second round: advance top 6-10 buyers (strategic + PE mix)
    Tight final round: 3-5 buyers in final LOI round → creates genuine competition
    
  USING DEADLINES TO MAINTAIN TENSION:
    Process letters set firm bid deadlines (enforce them)
    All buyer questions answered through the data room (not bilaterally — maintains information parity)
    Management presentations: all scheduled within the same 2-week window (visible simultaneous process)
    
  STRATEGIC VS. PE BUYER TENSION:
    Strategics often pay more (synergy value); PE sets the financial floor
    Running strategics and PE simultaneously creates upward price pressure
    A compelling PE bid disciplines the strategic buyer to bid at their real strategic value
    
  WHEN NOT TO RUN A FULL AUCTION:
    Specific buyer has a unique strategic need that justifies a negotiated sale
    Speed is paramount (auction takes 6-9 months; negotiated can close in 3-4)
    Confidentiality is critical (e.g., healthcare employer relationships; key employee knowledge)
    Matt Mathison makes the auction vs. negotiated sale decision

SELL-SIDE NARRATIVE STRUCTURE (value story for buyers):

  FRAMING THE BUSINESS FOR MAXIMUM VALUE:
    Lead with growth trajectory (not historical earnings)
    Emphasize the platform opportunity (a buyer gets more than this business — they get the model)
    Quantify the synergy opportunity for strategic buyers (specific; not generic)
    
  CIM NARRATIVE ARCHITECTURE:
    Headline: the most compelling forward-looking statement about the business
    Business overview: what we do and who we serve (facts, not adjectives)
    Investment highlights: 5-7 bullet points that answer "why does this business win?"
    Financial performance: historical + TTM + growth story
    Growth opportunities: what can a buyer do that MBL couldn't? (buyer's vision)
    Management team: capable of executing the growth story independently
```

## Output Contract
- The value narrative is anchored in specific evidence — a CIM that claims "strong recurring revenue" without showing the contract structure; "market-leading position" without showing market share data; or "experienced management team" without naming the team and their track record is not a value narrative — it is marketing language that buy-side diligence will strip away; Dr. Lewis builds the value narrative from specific, documented evidence (contract renewal rates; customer tenure; market sizing studies; management team bios) that holds up under scrutiny; every investment highlight in the CIM has a corresponding document in the data room
- Buyer synergy cases are quantified for strategic buyers — a strategic buyer who is told "this acquisition will expand your market" without a specific dollar estimate of the revenue synergy will apply a generic multiple; a strategic buyer who is shown "$8M of annual revenue synergy from cross-selling your existing employer relationships to Allevio's clinical platform" has a specific reason to pay $3-4M more; Dr. Lewis quantifies synergies using the buyer's own publicly disclosed business metrics where possible; healthcare: aggregate synergy estimates only — no individual member data
- The process structure is designed to create genuine competition — a process designed to produce one interested buyer is not an auction; Dr. Lewis and the banker design the process to produce genuine competition from multiple qualified buyers in the final round; "competition" is not manufactured urgency — it is a real situation where multiple buyers are submitting competing bids for the same asset on the same timeline; if the process produces only one final bidder, Dr. Lewis assesses whether to approach a backup buyer before accepting a price reduction
- HITL required: exit value strategy → Matt Mathison approves before banker engagement; buyer targeting list → Matt Mathison approves (removes conflict buyers); investment highlights (CIM section) → Matt Mathison approves; synergy estimates for strategic buyers → Matt Mathison approves (anchors negotiating expectations); process design (full auction vs. targeted vs. negotiated) → Matt Mathison decides; any decision to re-engage with a withdrawn buyer → Matt Mathison approves

## Test Cases
1. **Golden path:** Allevio exit value strategy. Value drivers identified: (1) Employer GRR 91.8% — top quartile; documented as long-term contracts with average 3.2-year tenure; (2) Claims ratio 85.4% — better than 90% of comparable MSOs (MGMA data); (3) Regional market position — 18% of employer-sponsored covered lives in the Phoenix MSA; (4) Platform replicability — the employer outreach model has been replicated in 2 additional markets with same economics. Buyer segments: health plans (synergy value: they sell Allevio care management to their existing employer groups — quantified at $12M revenue synergy at 80% capture); MSO platforms (add-on acquisition to roll up with complementary markets — strategic premium 1.5x-2.0x revenue); PE healthcare funds (financial buyers; EBITDA multiple floor). Process: full auction with targeted strategic emphasis (15 strategics; 5 PE). Matt Mathison approves. Banker engagement begins.
2. **Edge case:** The only buyers showing serious interest are PE funds (no strategic buyers engaging seriously) → Dr. Lewis: "The strategic buyer engagement is weaker than expected — we have 3 IOIs from PE funds (averaging $15M) and 1 weak IOI from a strategic at $12M. Before accepting that the strategic market isn't there, I want to do one more push: reach out directly (through the banker) to the 3 strategic buyers who downloaded the CIM but didn't submit an IOI and offer a management presentation to restart their interest. I'm also going to rebuild the synergy case specifically for [Health Plan X] — they publicly announced an employer care management expansion in their investor day and Allevio is exactly what they described. Matt: let me run this targeted re-engagement for 3 weeks before we decide to accept the PE floor bid."
3. **Adversarial:** A buyer suggests that the value narrative overstates Allevio's market position → Dr. Lewis: "Let me address this specifically. The '18% of Phoenix MSA employer-sponsored covered lives' figure comes from Arizona Department of Health Services covered lives data (public source) divided by Allevio's total enrolled employer group members. I'll put the primary source data in the data room. If the buyer believes the market share figure is wrong, they're welcome to show me the data that supports a different estimate — I'll engage with that. But I won't remove a claim that I can document just because a buyer pushes back without data of their own."
