# Change Communication Planner

Plan communications for a major organizational change — a restructuring, layoff, acquisition, leadership change, or significant policy shift. Change communications done wrong turn manageable transitions into morale crises. Done well, they preserve trust, maintain performance, and position leadership as honest and human. The difference is preparation.

## When to Use
- A portfolio company is restructuring, right-sizing, or eliminating roles
- A CEO or senior leader is departing or being replaced
- An acquisition closes and the acquired company's employees need to be addressed
- A significant policy change (benefits, hybrid work, compensation structure) is being implemented
- Matt Mathison asks "how do we communicate this without making it worse?"

## Change Communication Principles

```
1. Tell people what you know — and what you don't
   Uncertainty is okay. Silence is not. "I don't know yet" is an answer.
   "Here's what I know, here's what I don't know yet, here's when I'll know more."

2. Lead with empathy, follow with information
   In change communications, people first need to feel heard before they 
   can process information. Open with acknowledgment, then deliver the news.

3. Direct report notification first
   Managers must know before their teams. A manager who finds out at the 
   same time as their team cannot support them. Never skip the manager cascade.

4. One spokesperson, one message
   If multiple people are communicating about the same change and saying 
   different things, trust collapses. One message, one voice, one source of truth.

5. Follow through
   Every commitment made in change communication must be kept. "We'll have 
   more information by Friday" means Friday, not Monday.
```

## Change Type Guide

```
Layoff / RIF (Reduction in Force):
  HITL: MANDATORY — Matt Mathison approval required before any action
  Legal: Employment counsel required BEFORE notification
  Sequence: Affected employees first (private, in-person/video) → Survivors (same day) → 
            External (only if needed; "reduction in force" not "layoffs" unless unavoidable)
  Timing: Announce and separate on the same day; do not tell people they're laid off 
          and then have them work 2 more weeks of uncertainty
  
Acquisition (acquired company employees):
  HITL: MANDATORY — Matt Mathison and portfolio company CEO
  Sequence: Leadership → Full team → Customers (if affected)
  Key message: Job security first ("Your job is [secure / being evaluated — specifics]")
  
Leadership change (CEO replacement, departure):
  HITL: MANDATORY — Matt Mathison approval
  Sequence: Board → Senior leadership → All employees → External/press
  Key message: Continuity + gratitude (departing) + confidence (successor)
  
Restructuring (org redesign, role changes):
  HITL: Dr. Lewis + Matt Mathison + employment counsel
  Sequence: Affected managers → Affected employees (individual) → Team → All company
  Key message: Strategic rationale + individual impact + transition plan
```

## Affected Employee Notification Script

*[For layoffs/restructuring — CEO or manager delivers this in a private 1:1. Not an email. Not a group.]*

> "[Name], I wanted to speak with you directly before you hear anything through other channels. I have difficult news. [Describe the change specifically — "your position is being eliminated" NOT "we're making some changes"]. Your last day will be [date]. 

> Here's what you'll receive: [severance details — weeks, continuation of benefits, COBRA, outplacement support if applicable].

> I want to be clear: this decision is about [the business decision — role elimination, restructuring] and not about your performance or the quality of your work.

> [If severance agreement required]: There is a severance agreement I need to ask you to review. You have [N] days — I'd encourage you to review it with an attorney.

> Is there anything you need right now, or any questions I can answer?"

---

## Surviving Employee Communication

*[All-hands or email — same day as individual notifications]*

Subject: An important update from [CEO name]

> Team,

> I want to share some important news about changes at [Company] and explain why we're making them.

> [What changed — describe clearly and specifically, without jargon]

> [Why — the honest business rationale. Not "to position us for growth" unless that's genuinely true and specific. "We need to reduce costs to reach profitability" is honest. "Strategic realignment" is not.]

> [Who is affected — if there have been role eliminations: "X members of our team are leaving today. This was a difficult decision and these are talented people who contributed to this company."]

> [What doesn't change — what is stable; what the team can count on continuing]

> [What comes next — the plan going forward; the leadership team's commitment]

> I know this creates questions. Here's what I know and what I don't know yet:
> **I know:** [List of known facts]
> **I'm still working on:** [Honest list of open items — with timing on when you'll know]

> Thank you for your continued work during a time that I know is difficult. I am available for questions — please reach out to me directly.

> [CEO name]

## Output Contract
- Legal clearance is a hard gate — no change communication plan is distributed and no individual notification occurs until employment counsel has reviewed; this is non-negotiable, especially for layoffs
- Individual notifications before company-wide — every person who is affected must be told individually before the company-wide announcement; discovering you've been laid off via a company email is a dignity violation that creates legal risk; no exceptions
- "I don't know" is an acceptable answer — it is far better than speculation or false assurance; every change communication must explicitly separate what is known from what is not
- Manager cascade is always included — managers who learn at the same time as their teams cannot support their teams; always brief managers 24 hours before
- HITL required: Employment counsel required before any layoff notification; Matt Mathison approval required before any restructuring or leadership change announcement; CEO delivers affected employee notifications personally (not HR alone); no communication distributed without explicit Matt Mathison and legal clearance

## System Dependencies
- **Reads from:** HR and legal cleared decisions, affected employee list, severance terms, employment counsel guidance
- **Writes to:** Change communication plan (SharePoint/Comms/Change Communications); manager scripts; company-wide communication; individual notification scripts
- **HITL Required:** Employment counsel before any notification; Matt Mathison approval before communication; CEO delivers affected notifications; all communications logged

## Test Cases
1. **Golden path:** Allevio eliminates 3 billing roles after RCM automation → Employment counsel reviews (no WARN Act trigger — fewer than 50 at single site); severance: 4 weeks + 60-day COBRA subsidy; T-1 day: 3 managers notified at 4pm; T-0 9am: 3 individual notifications (private video calls, CEO + manager each); 11am: surviving team company-wide email from CEO with clear rationale (automation, not performance); FAQ document provided; no press release needed; team performance stable within 2 weeks
2. **Edge case:** A manager leaks the layoff to the affected employee before the official notification → immediately escalate to Matt Mathison and employment counsel; the affected employee's official notification must still occur for legal documentation purposes; address the manager's breach of confidentiality separately; note this in the change communication debrief to improve manager cascade protocols
3. **Adversarial:** An employee who was let go posts angrily on LinkedIn about the layoff → do not respond publicly; route to social-media-crisis-responder; a factual, aggressive public response makes it worse; a private, human outreach to the former employee may be appropriate (not to suppress the post, but to address their concern genuinely); legal counsel advises on any legal claims in the post
